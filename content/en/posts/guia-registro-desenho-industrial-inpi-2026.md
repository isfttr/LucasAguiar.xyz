The Industrial Design (ID) registration protects the ornamental appearance of an object — the three-dimensional shape of a package, a piece of furniture, a toy, or two-dimensional aspects such as a fabric print or the decorative pattern of a ceramic. Unlike a patent (which protects the technical function) or a trademark (which protects the commercial identity), the ID protects the product's **visual design**.

In Brazil, registration is granted by the INPI and regulated by the Industrial Property Law (Law No. 9.279/96), articles 95 to 106. This guide covers the entire process: what can and cannot be registered, costs, documents, step-by-step electronic filing, deadlines, and the international route through the Hague Agreement.

> **Updated July 2026:** on 06/26/2026, the INPI published the most recent update to the Industrial Designs Manual, with changes in document copies, proof of unionist priority, and compliance petitions. The e-DI system continues normal operation.

## What is an Industrial Design?

**Article 95 of the IPL** defines industrial design as "the ornamental plastic form of an object or the ornamental set of lines and colors that can be applied to a product, providing a new and original visual result in its external configuration and that can serve as a type of industrial manufacture."

In practice, the ID protects:

| What it registers | Examples |
|----------------|----------|
| **Three-dimensional shape** | Design of a bottle, chair, smartphone, jewelry, lamp |
| **Two-dimensional pattern** | Fabric print, wallpaper pattern, graphics applied to a surface |
| **Ornamental set** | Combination of shape + pattern on the same product |
| **Part of a product** | Handle of a bag, base of an appliance (as long as visible and ornamental) |

**What CANNOT be registered as an ID:**

- Purely artistic works (sculptures, paintings — protected by copyright)
- Technical or functional aspects (protected by invention patent or utility model)
- Distinctive signs (protected as trademarks)
- Engineering works or architectural projects
- Shapes necessary for a technical effect (e.g., mandatory aerodynamic shape)

> ⚠️ **Important:** if the shape of a product is determined exclusively by its technical function, it **cannot** be registered as an industrial design. This prevents so-called "evergreening" of functional designs.

## Industrial Design vs. Other Protections

It is common to confuse the types of protection. The table below compares the main differences:

| Aspect | Industrial Design | Invention Patent | Utility Model | Trademark |
|---------|-------------------|--------------------|--------------------|-------|
| **What it protects** | Ornamental appearance | New and inventive invention | Functional improvement | Commercial identity |
| **Merit examination** | Yes (novelty + originality) | Yes (inventive step) | Yes (inventive act) | Yes (distinctiveness) |
| **Validity term** | 10 years + 3 renewals of 5 years = **25 years** | 20 years | 15 years | 10 years (renewable indefinitely) |
| **Novelty examination** | Worldwide (state of the art) | Worldwide | Worldwide | National (conflict) |
| **Average grant time** | 6-12 months | 5-10 years (with backlog) | 3-5 years | 12-18 months |
| **Publication** | Automatic (18 months) | Automatic (18 months) | Automatic (18 months) | After formal examination |

The ID is generally the **fastest and cheapest** way to protect the appearance of an industrialized product.

## Legal Requirements: Novelty and Originality

To be registered, the industrial design must meet two requirements:

**1. Novelty (Art. 96 IPL):** the design must not be comprised in the state of the art. The state of the art is everything that has been made accessible to the public before the filing date, in Brazil or abroad, by written or oral description, use, or any other means.

**2. Originality (Art. 97 IPL):** the design must result in a distinctive visual configuration compared to previous objects. Inventive step (as in patents) is not required, but the visual result must not be merely trivial or arising from obvious combinations of known elements.

### Grace Period (Art. 96, §2 IPL)

The inventor may disclose their design **up to 180 days before filing** without losing novelty, provided the disclosure was made by the inventor themselves, by third parties based on the inventor's information, or by the INPI in an official publication. This period is important for fairs, exhibitions, and commercial presentations.

## Required Documents

To file an ID registration application with the INPI, you need:

1. **Reproductions of the industrial design** — figures or photographs showing the object from all necessary angles (perspective, front, side, top, bottom). The Industrial Designs Manual (item 5.5) details presentation rules:
   - Format: JPEG or PNG
   - Minimum resolution: 300 DPI
   - Neutral background (preferably white)
   - Hatching lines to indicate transparency or materials
2. **Descriptive report** (optional for ID, but recommended) — describes what each view represents
3. **Power of attorney** — if filed by an attorney/lawyer
4. **Proof of payment of the GRU** — with the number generated in the e-INPI system
5. **Priority declaration** — if claiming unionist priority (6-month term from foreign filing)

> **Three-dimensional vs. two-dimensional designs:** the INPI handles them differently. Three-dimensional designs require multiple views (usually 6: perspective, front, back, left side, right side, top, base). Two-dimensional designs (prints) may require only a front view, with indication of rapport (repetition) when applicable.

## Step-by-Step Registration

### Step 1: Registration in e-INPI

Before any procedure, you need to register in [e-INPI](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/e-inpi), the INPI's electronic services system. Individuals use a gov.br account (silver or gold level). Legal entities need an ICP-Brasil digital certificate from the legal representative.

See the [guide to accessing CNPJ with a gov.br account]({{< relref "posts/inpi-acesso-cnpj-conta-govbr-guia-2026/" >}}) for more details.

### Step 2: Issue and pay the GRU

Access the [GRU Issuance System](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/desenhos-industriais/custos-e-pagamento) and issue the Federal Collection Guide for the desired fee code (filing = 300, examination = 301, etc.).

- Payment is made via barcode at any bank
- Keep the GRU number — it will be required in the filing
- The GRU expires in 30 days (do not confuse with the legal deadline of the process)

### Step 3: File the application in e-DI

After paying the GRU, access the [e-DI system](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/desenhos-industriais) and:

1. Select "New ID Registration Application"
2. Enter the paid GRU number
3. Fill in the applicant's and author's data (they can be different people)
4. Upload the figures/photographs of the design
5. If claiming unionist priority, attach the priority document
6. Confirm and submit

### Step 4: Formal examination

The INPI performs the formal examination to verify that the documentation is complete and correct. If there are irregularities, the INPI publishes an **office action** in the RPI — you have 60 days (extendable by another 60) to comply.

### Step 5: Merit examination

The INPI examines novelty and originality based on the worldwide state of the art. If approved, the registration is granted and published in the RPI for the knowledge of third parties.

### Step 6: Certificate

After grant and payment of the certificate fee (code 310), the INPI issues the industrial design registration certificate.

**Estimated total timeframe:** 6 to 18 months, significantly faster than patents (which can take 5-10 years).

## Validity Term and Maintenance

The ID registration has an initial term of **10 years** from the filing date, renewable for **three successive periods of 5 years**, totaling up to **25 years** of protection.

| Period | Action | Fee |
|---------|------|------|
| Years 1-5 | Initial maintenance fee (already included in filing) | — |
| Year 5 | Five-year maintenance/renewal | Code 305 |
| Year 10 | 1st renewal for 5 years | Code 305 |
| Year 15 | 2nd renewal for 5 years | Code 305 |
| Year 20 | 3rd renewal for 5 years | Code 305 |

> ⚠️ **The maintenance fee (code 305) must be paid every 5 years, counted from the filing date.** Non-payment results in extinction of the registration. The INPI sends reminders through the "My Requests" system.

## Hague Agreement: International Registration

Brazil joined the **Hague Agreement for the International Registration of Industrial Designs** in 2023, administered by WIPO. This allows a single international application to protect the design in up to **96 countries** (all contracting parties of the Agreement).

### Advantages of the Hague Agreement

- **Single application** in one language (English, French, or Spanish)
- **Unified fees** in Swiss francs (CHF)
- Up to **100 designs** per application (same Locarno class)
- **Minimum 15 years** of international protection (renewable up to the maximum of the designated country)
- In Brazil, the term via Hague also reaches **25 years**

### How to file via Hague

1. Access [eHague](https://www.wipo.int/hague/en/eHague/) — WIPO's online platform
2. Complete the form and upload the reproductions
3. Designate the countries where you seek protection (include Brazil if you want national protection)
4. Pay the fees: basic + publication + designation per country
5. WIPO examines the formal requirements and publishes in the International Designs Bulletin

Each designated country may conduct its own substantive examination — the INPI has 6 months to refuse protection in Brazil based on national legislation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a lawyer to register?

It is not mandatory for individuals or legal entities. The e-DI system is self-instructional. However, an IP agent can help with drafting the descriptive report and the international protection strategy. If you are abroad without a domicile in Brazil, you need to appoint a local attorney.

### Can I register a design I am already selling?

Yes, as long as the sale occurred **less than 180 days ago** (grace period of Art. 96, §2 of the IPL). After that period, the design loses novelty.

### Does the ID registration cover the entire product or just the design?

Only the appearance. If a competitor copies your packaging but uses a different shape, there may be no infringement. Therefore, for innovative products, one combines ID + patent (for function) + trademark (for identity).

### What is the difference between ID and Copyright?

ID protects the design applied to industry (mass-produced object). Copyright protects the work as an aesthetic creation, independent of industrial application. In Brazil, cumulative protection is possible: the design of a piece of jewelry can be registered as an ID and also protected by copyright.

### Does ID cover digital designs (UI/UX)?

User interface (GUI) can be protected as ID in Brazil if applied to an industrial product — for example, the visual layout of an app when displayed on a smartphone screen. The INPI has already granted ID registrations for GUIs, provided industrial application is demonstrated.

## What changed in the Industrial Designs Manual (June 2026)

On June 26, 2026, the INPI published the most recent update to the ID Manual. The main changes:

1. **Document copies:** clearer guidance on how to request copies of ID process documents, with a standardized procedure
2. **Proof of unionist priority obtained by assignment:** new documentary requirements when the priority right is acquired by assignment from third parties
3. **Compliance petitions:** adjustments to forms for responding to INPI office actions

The update is specific but important for those dealing with unionist priority (filing abroad followed by filing in Brazil within 6 months).

## Conclusion

Industrial design registration is a strategic and underutilized tool in Brazil. With a grant timeframe much faster than patents, affordable costs (especially with the 70% discount for individuals and SMEs), and the possibility of international protection via the Hague Agreement, the ID should be on the radar of any company that invests in product design.

The INPI's update to the ID Manual in June 2026 shows that the agency is adjusting its procedures to keep up with the growing volume of applications — driven by the accession to the Hague Agreement in 2023 and increased awareness of the importance of industrial design as an intellectual property asset.
