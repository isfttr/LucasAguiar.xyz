In January 2026, the [INPI discontinued the "backlogometer"](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/patentes/plano-de-combate-ao-backlog) — the public dashboard that monitored the reduction in the backlog of pending patent applications — justifying that the project achieved its objective. For the general universe of applications, the statement is technically correct. For pharmaceutical patents, the reality is quite different.

## What was the Backlog Combat Plan

Launched in July 2019 by [INPI/PR Resolutions No. 240 and 241](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/patentes/plano-de-combate-ao-backlog), the plan started with a backlog of **149,912 pending applications** filed until 2016. The central strategy was to use prior art searches already carried out by foreign offices (USPTO, EPO) to issue preliminary requirements (dispatch 6.21), forcing the applicant to respond within 90 days or have the application shelved.

The results were impressive: a 55% reduction in the backlog by March 2021 and the achievement of the official target of 80% by the end of the same year. The technical staff was reinforced with 210 new examiners and automated search tools.

## The blind spot: pharmaceuticals were left out

Pharmaceutical applications subject to [art. 229-C of Law 9,279/1996 (IPL)](https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/leis/l9279.htm) — which required prior ANVISA approval until its extinction by [Law 14,195/2021](https://www.planalto.gov.br/ccivil_03/_ato2019-2022/2021/lei/L14195.htm) — were **explicitly excluded from the Plan**. This exclusion has practical consequences to this day: a survey by [Interfarma](https://www.interfarma.org.br/requerente-de-patente-no-brasil-espera-mais-para-ter-pedido-concedido-aponta-levantamento/) points to an average time of **10.25 years** between filing and grant for biopharmaceuticals in the 2020-2022 period, against a general average that dropped to approximately 4 years after the plan.

INPI's total residual backlog still exceeds **115,000 pending applications**, according to the [Monthly IP Bulletin of December 2024](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/inpi-data/dados-e-series-temporais).

## What changes with Ordinance No. 110/2025

The [GM/MDIC Ordinance No. 110/2025](https://www.legisweb.com.br/legislacao/?id=477842), signed on 05/05/2025 and published in the DOU on 09/05/2025, institutes the update for the Table of Fees - a document that lists all services provided by INPI. The changes **do not institute automatic grant without substantive examination** — a narrative that circulated mistakenly. According to art. 3, the issuance of the patent letter is now exempt from a separate petition. Previously, the applicant needed to file a petition, along with payment, counting from the decision to grant the patent application.

> Art. 3, I:

> a. Issuance of patent letter or certificate of addition of invention (petition waived) - within the ordinary term (code 212);

> b. Issuance of patent letter or certificate of addition of invention (petition waived) - within the extraordinary term (code 213);

Now the applicant pays a single fee at the beginning that already includes the future issuance of the patent letter. Concurrently, the average fee adjustment was 24.1%, with reductions of up to 60% for micro-enterprises, EPPs, and ICTs, and total exemption for CadÚnico beneficiaries and PwDs.

The ordinance does not change the substantive examination procedure nor does it create exceptions for pharmaceutical applications.

## Why this matters to those filing patents today

The drop in the volume of pharmaceutical decisions — from 6,400 in 2021 to 2,600 by September 2024, according to [Interfarma survey reported by Futuro da Saúde](https://futurodasaude.com.br/registro-de-patentes-farmaceuticas/) — demonstrates a specific bottleneck in the sector. It is the predictable result of three structural factors that the Backlog Combat Plan did not address in the sector: chronic shortage of specialized examiners (only 23 in pharmaceuticals and 28 in biopharmaceuticals), increasing technical complexity of biotechnology applications, and the absence of acceleration mechanisms for this segment.

In 2025, the data from the [IDS IP Bulletin](https://ids.org.br/noticia/inpi-divulga-boletim-de-propriedade-industrial-com-panorama-estatistico-dos-pedidos-e-concessoes-no-ano-de-2025/) show partial recovery — filings increased by 6.7% (29,557) and total grants grew by 5.5% (13,624). Brazil surpassed the USA as the largest origin of applications (26.2% vs 23.5%). These are positive signs and demonstrate that there is an appetite for innovation in Brazil. However, the bottleneck observed in the pharmaceutical sector still remains a challenge.

![Comparison of the number of patents filed and granted in 2024 and 2025, respectively.](https://lucasaguiarxyzstorage.blob.core.windows.net/images/comparativo_patentes_inpi_2024_2025.png)

The [automation that INPI plans for the 2025-2029 roadmap]({{< ref "posts/inpi-automation-roadmap-2025-2029.md" >}}) — including the use of AI as an examination assistant with a decision target within two years — could be the structural step that was missing from the original plan.

Meanwhile, the debate on how to compensate for historical delays in the effective term of pharmaceutical patents gained a new chapter in Congress through the [discussion of Patent Term Adjustment (PTA)]({{< ref "posts/propostas_pta_patentes_2026.md" >}}), which aims to adjust the term of pharmaceutical patents based on the waiting time for obtaining the grant of the application by INPI.
