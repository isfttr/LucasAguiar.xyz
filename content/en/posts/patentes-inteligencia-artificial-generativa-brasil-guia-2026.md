Generative AI (GenAI) patent filings have more than doubled in two years: from approximately 14,000 patent families published in 2023 to over 37,800 in 2025. Combined, 2024 and 2025 saw more than 56,000 new GenAI patent families — exceeding the entire previous decade (2014–2023), according to data released in July 2026 by the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO).

GenAI now accounts for 8.7% of all AI-related patent family publications, up from 6.1% in 2023 and 4.2% in 2017. This surge, driven by multinational enterprises across finance, telecommunications, and infrastructure, raises a practical question for inventors, companies, and IP firms in Brazil: **how do you patent a generative AI invention at the INPI?**

This guide covers the legal framework, patentability rules, international classifications, latest global data, and best practices for filing GenAI patent applications in Brazil.

## Patentability of AI Inventions in Brazil: What the LPI Says

In Brazil, patentability of generative AI inventions is governed by the **Industrial Property Law (LPI)** — Law 9,279/96. The critical issue is the distinction between what is and is not considered a patentable invention.

### What the LPI Excludes (Article 10)

Art. 10 of the LPI lists what is **not** considered an invention or utility model. The most relevant provisions for AI are:

- **Item I** — discoveries, scientific theories, and mathematical methods
- **Item II** — purely abstract concepts
- **Item V** — **computer programs per se** (protected by copyright under Law 9,609/98)
- **Item VIII** — surgical, therapeutic, or diagnostic techniques and methods

This means a **purely mathematical algorithm** or an **AI model presented in the abstract** (e.g., a transformer architecture without a technical application) **is not patentable** in Brazil. Nor is "computer software per se" — the source code of the model is not patent subject matter.

### What Is Patentable (Article 8)

Art. 8 requires three conditions:
- **Novelty** (art. 11)
- **Inventive step** (art. 13)
- **Industrial application** (art. 15)

For AI inventions, the key is demonstrating that the application does not claim an abstract algorithm, but rather a **technical solution** to a **technical problem** that produces a **technical effect** and has **industrial application**.

### INPI's Practice for Computer-Implemented Inventions

The INPI follows the concept of **computer-implemented inventions (CII)** in its examination practice. An AI invention is patentable when:

1. It is **tied to a concrete technical application** — describing the mathematical model alone is insufficient; it must solve a specific technical problem (e.g., image compression, speech synthesis, industrial process optimization)
2. It produces a **technical effect** — the result goes beyond merely processing data or generating content; there is a transformation in the state of the art
3. It does not claim **the computer program per se** — claims must be directed to the method, system, or use, not the code

In practice, GenAI patent applications with the highest approval chances at the INPI describe:

- **Methods** of training or inference with a specific technical application
- **Systems** implementing AI models to solve industrial problems
- **Uses** of generative models in technical processes (e.g., protein sequence generation for enzyme engineering, integrated circuit layout optimization)
- **Neural network architectures** with demonstrated structural innovation

## How to File an AI Patent Application at the INPI

The process follows the standard patent of invention (PI) filing procedure, with specific considerations for AI inventions:

### 1. Prior Art Search

Before filing, search international databases (USPTO, EPO, WIPO Patentscope) and the INPI database. For GenAI, typical keywords include:
- "generative adversarial network", "transformer", "diffusion model", "large language model"
- IPC codes: G06N 3/00, G06N 20/00
- Combine with the technical application (e.g., G06N 3/04 + G06T 11/00 for image generation)

### 2. Drafting the Application

Claim drafting is the most critical step for GenAI. Claims should:

- **Avoid purely mathematical language** — claims describing only mathematical operations without connection to a technical application are rejected under art. 10, I
- **Describe the technical problem** — what limitation in the prior art does the invention overcome?
- **Claim the application, not the model** — instead of "a large language model configured to generate text," claim "a method for automatic summarization of technical documents using a large language model"
- **Include system and method claims** — independent claims for both the method and the system increase protection breadth

### 4. Examination and Timelines

The INPI released new patent module features in 2026 that facilitate process monitoring. AI patents follow general examination timelines:

- Application publication: 18 months from filing (or priority date)
- Examination request: up to 36 months from filing
- Average examination time (2025–2026): 5–7 years (subject to backlog)
- Priority examination (PPH/GPPH): can reduce to 1–2 years

## Strategies to Increase Grant Chances

Based on INPI's examination practice and precedents for software and AI patents, these strategies improve grant prospects:

### 1. Connect AI to a Specific Industrial Application

The LPI requires industrial application (art. 15). For GenAI, demonstrate that the invention can be **produced or used in any type of industry**:
- Image generation for automated visual inspection in manufacturing
- Molecule synthesis for novel materials discovery
- Logistics process optimization with reinforcement learning
- Code generation for automated software testing

### 2. Include Comparative Data

Comparative data demonstrating **measurable improvement** over the prior art (accuracy, speed, memory consumption, perceptual quality) strengthens the inventive step argument.

### 3. Describe Architecture with Sufficient Technical Detail

The INPI requires the description to be clear enough for a person skilled in the art to reproduce the invention. For AI models, this includes:
- Number of layers and parameters (when relevant)
- Activation and loss functions
- Training hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size, epochs)
- Training dataset (at minimum, type and provenance)
- Evaluation metrics

### 4. Avoid the "Computer Program Per Se" Exclusion

The greatest risk of rejection for AI patents in Brazil is classification under art. 10, V (computer program per se). To avoid this:
- **Do not** use language like "a software configured to..."
- **Do not** claim source code or the algorithm in the abstract
- **Do** use language like "a computer-implemented method for..."
- **Do** connect each method step to a technical component (processor, memory, sensor, actuator)

## Brazil's Position in the Global Landscape

Brazil does not yet rank among the top GenAI patent filers — the national AI IP ecosystem is nascent compared to China, the US, Japan, and Europe. However, the country has structural advantages:

- **Innovation Law (Law 10,973/2004)** — allows universities and research institutes (ICTs) to file patents and negotiate licensing with companies
- **Budapest Treaty** — Brazil is a signatory, facilitating patent deposits involving biological material (relevant for GenAI in synthetic biology)
- **Backlog Reduction Program** — the INPI has been reducing pending applications, and AI patents may benefit from priority examination via PPH
- **Lei do Bem (Law 11,196/2005)** — tax incentives for R&D that can cover AI invention development

## Conclusion: What to Watch

The GenAI patent boom is structural — not a speculative bubble. Companies like SoftBank, Tencent, and Alphabet are building defensive and offensive positions in what WIPO calls "one of the fastest-moving fields of innovation ever observed." For Brazilian inventors and companies, the time is right to:

1. **Map** AI inventions developed internally that may be protectable
2. **Assess** patentability of each invention under art. 10 of the LPI
3. **Draft** applications focused on technical application and technical effect — not abstract algorithms
4. **File** first at the INPI and consider PCT extension for international protection
5. **Monitor** regulatory developments and INPI's evolving examination guidelines for AI

GenAI represents not just a technological frontier, but a legal one. The INPI and patent offices worldwide are developing their jurisprudence in real time. Those who file now — with well-drafted applications — will be ahead when the system matures.
