The INPI has published the 2026 edition of the report **"Brazil in the Global Intangible Investment Landscape"**, produced in partnership with the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO/OMPI) and the Luiss Business School. The study arrives on the same day WIPO announced that global investment in intangible assets — software, data, brands, R&D, organizational capital — **surpassed US$ 10 trillion for the first time in 2025**.

The two documents, which complement each other, offer the most complete picture ever produced of how intangible value is being generated and measured in Brazil and worldwide.

## The Global Landscape: US$ 10 Trillion and Accelerated Growth

The third edition of the **World Intangible Investment Highlights** (WIPO/LBS, 2026) covers 29 high- and middle-income economies, accounting for about 57% of global GDP. The numbers are significant:

| Indicator | Value | Period |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| Global investment in intangibles | **US$ 10 trillion** | 2025 |
| Annual growth (intangibles) | **5.5%** | 2020–2025 |
| Annual growth (tangibles) | **3.2%** | 2020–2025 |
| Share of GDP in covered economies | **~13%** | 2025 |
| US investment | **~US$ 5 trillion** | 2025 |
| Japan | **US$ 810 billion** | 2024 |
| Germany | **US$ 695 billion** | 2024 |

The United States accounts for roughly half of the global total. The gap between the US and the next four largest economies combined has nearly doubled over the past decade, from US$ 1 trillion (2015) to US$ 2 trillion (2025). Japan rose to the second global position with 4.8% growth in intangible investments in 2024, while its tangible investments stagnate.

## Brazil in the Ranking: 7th Largest Economy in Intangibles

The INPI report ranks Brazil as the **seventh largest economy in the world in absolute investment in intangible assets**, with US$ 312 billion invested in 2023:

- Value close to Canada and just below Italy
- Ahead of Spain, the Netherlands, Sweden, and India
- Equivalent to **7.6% of Brazilian GDP**
- Scale comparable to the agricultural sector (6.0% of GDP) and above the extractive industry (3.7%)

In **brands**, Brazil is the **fifth largest economy in the world** in absolute investment (US$ 79 billion), surpassing France, Italy, and Spain. Brands account for 25% of Brazilian intangible investment — a larger share than the United Kingdom.

### Distribution of Brazilian Intangible Investment

| Asset Type | Highlight |
|--------------|----------|
| Brands | US$ 79 billion (5th in the world, 25% of total) |
| Software and data | Included in the WIIH calculation |
| R&D | Partially captured in National Accounts |
| Organizational capital | Not captured (largest measurement gap) |

## The Measurement Problem: 72% Outside National Accounts

One of the most relevant findings of the INPI report is that **about 72% of Brazilian intangible investment is not captured by the National Accounts**. This is not an error — it reflects an international accounting convention that treats spending on brands, organizational capital, and training as current expenses, not as investment.

The same pattern of underestimation appears, to varying degrees, in the 29 economies covered by the study. The practical consequence is that the GDP and productivity of intangible-intensive economies — such as Brazil already is — may be **systematically underestimated**.

The **Global INTAN-Invest** database, maintained by the WIPO-Luiss Business School partnership, seeks to fill this gap by following the methodology of Corrado, Hulten and Sichel (2005, 2009), which includes both measured and unmeasured assets in official statistics.

## The Two Waves of AI Investment

The WIPO report highlights how artificial intelligence is accelerating investment through two distinct waves:

**1. Tangible infrastructure** — data centers, semiconductors, energy systems, and networks. This wave is strong but geographically concentrated (mainly in the US).

**2. Intangible** — data, software, R&D, brands, organizational capital, and training. The report argues that the lasting economic impact of AI will come less from physical infrastructure and more from the intangible assets built upon it.

As Cecilia Jona-Lasinio, professor at Luiss Business School and co-author of the report, notes: *"AI is not just a new technology. It is changing how knowledge itself is produced, making intangible investment the key to understanding its economic impact."*

For Brazil, which already invests US$ 312 billion in intangibles, the question is how to accelerate the second wave — that of assets based on data, software, and organizational innovation — so as not to be left with only imported infrastructure.

## Implications for Industrial Property Policy

The numbers in the INPI report have direct implications for Brazilian IP policy:

- **Brands as a strategic asset**: Brazil is already the 5th largest global investor in brands. The public consultation on position marks and adherence to the Madrid Protocol indicate that INPI is preparing the ground for a more sophisticated brand ecosystem.
- **Accounting underestimation**: if 72% of intangible investment does not appear in national accounts, industrial policy decisions based solely on traditional GDP may be underestimating the true weight of innovation in the economy.
- **Connection to open innovation**: the fact that brands represent 25% of Brazilian intangible investment (above the United Kingdom) suggests the country has an innovation profile oriented toward brands and the market, not just traditional R&D.
- **WIPO data as a guide**: Brazil appears in WIPO reports both in PCT patents, international trademarks, and now in intangible investment — these are three metrics that tell the same story of a maturing IP ecosystem.

## What to Follow

The report "Brazil in the Global Intangible Investment Landscape" is available on the INPI website, and the full **World Intangible Investment Highlights 2026** can be accessed on the WIPO portal. Both documents are expected to be updated annually.

For those working with IP, innovation, and industrial policy, this is a landmark: for the first time, Brazil has a systematic diagnosis of its intangible investment, aligned with international methodology. The next step is to see how this diagnosis translates into policies — in the LPI reform, INPI planning, and the Brazilian industrial strategy.
