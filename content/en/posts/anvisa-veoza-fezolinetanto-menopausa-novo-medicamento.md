On June 23, 2026, the National Health Surveillance Agency (ANVISA) approved the registration of the drug Veoza™ (fezolinetant), a selective neurokinin-3 (NK3) receptor antagonist indicated for the treatment of moderate to severe vasomotor symptoms (VMS) associated with menopause — the popular "hot flashes" or hot waves.

This is the **first non-hormonal drug approved in Brazil** for this indication, representing an important alternative for patients who cannot or do not wish to undergo hormone replacement therapy (HRT).

## What is fezolinetant and how does it work?

Fezolinetant is a small-molecule, orally administered molecule that acts by blocking neurokinin B from binding to NK3 receptors in the brain. During menopause, the drop in estrogen levels dysregulates the balance between this hormone and neurokinin B, affecting the hypothalamic center for body temperature control. The result is sudden episodes of intense heat, sweating, and flushing, which significantly affect quality of life.

Unlike hormonal therapies, fezolinetant acts directly on the neurological mechanism of hot flashes, without interfering with systemic hormone levels. This reduces risks associated with HRT, such as an increased incidence of certain types of cancer and thromboembolic events.

Phase III clinical studies (SKYLIGHT 1 and 2) demonstrated that, **in four weeks of treatment, the drug reduced daily hot flashes by an average of 53%**, with a consistent safety profile. The most common adverse effects include abdominal pain, diarrhea, insomnia, back pain, and elevated liver transaminases — which is why the FDA added, in September 2024, a warning about a rare risk of severe liver injury.

## Global approval history

Fezolinetant was originally discovered by Ogeda (formerly Euroscreen), a Belgian biotechnology company, and acquired by Astellas Pharma in April 2017 for approximately €500 million (up to €820 million with development milestones). The regulatory path was fast for a first-in-class:

| Milestone | Date |
|-------|------|
| Acquisition by Astellas | April/2017 |
| Phase III Results (SKYLIGHT 1) | March/2023 (The Lancet) |
| FDA Approval (USA) | May/2023 |
| EMA Approval (European Union) | December/2023 |
| ANVISA Approval (Brazil) | June/2026 |

The approximately three-year interval between FDA and ANVISA approval is consistent with the average evaluation time for innovative drugs in Brazil, where there is no acceleration mechanism equivalent to the FDA's priority review. Resolution 2.430/2026, published in the Official Gazette of the Union, formalized the registration.

## Impact for the Brazilian pharmaceutical market

Veoza's approval comes at a time of increasing attention to women's health and population aging. According to the European Medicines Agency (EMA), the global prevalence of vasomotor symptoms associated with menopause is estimated between 11% and 47% in women over 40. In Brazil, with approximately 55 million women in this age group, the potential market is significant.

The arrival of a first-in-class non-hormonal treatment also raises strategic questions for the Brazilian pharmaceutical industry:

### Patent protection and access

Astellas holds patents on fezolinetant that protect the molecule and its use for treating vasomotor symptoms. In Brazil, the corresponding patent application was examined by the INPI — and the post-approval exclusivity period will depend on the extent of the current protection and the eventual submission of Patent of Interest requests for the Unified Health System (SUS).

The high expected cost of treatment (Veozah is marketed in the US for approximately US$ 550 monthly) raises questions about incorporation into the SUS and access strategies. The [specific backlog in the pharmaceutical sector at INPI]({{< relref "posts/backlog-inpi-patentes-farmaceuticas/" >}}) may influence the timing of generics and biosimilars entering the market in the future.

### Technovigilance regulation

ANVISA also recently opened a public call to improve technovigilance rules in Brazil, which may impact the post-market monitoring of drugs like fezolinetant, especially considering the hepatotoxicity warning included in the American package insert.

## What to watch for going forward

1.  **Price and incorporation**: What will Astellas' pricing strategy be in Brazil? Will the drug be incorporated into the SUS or remain restricted to the private market?
2.  **Patents and generic entry**: The patent protection period for fezolinetant in Brazil will define the exclusivity window — a critical factor for broad access.
3.  **Advertising regulation**: How will ANVISA treat the advertising of a menopause drug, especially considering the history of debates about the "medicalization" of the climacteric?
4.  **Pipeline competition**: Other pharmaceutical companies (such as Bayer and Mitsubishi Tanabe) have similar molecules in development — the approval of fezolinetant paves the way for an entire therapeutic class.

## Conclusion

The approval of Veoza by ANVISA marks the entry into Brazil of a new therapeutic class for a health problem affecting tens of millions of women. Beyond the immediate clinical benefit, the case illustrates important dynamics of Brazil's industrial property and health regulatory system: the approval time for first-in-class drugs, the balance between innovation and access, and the role of INPI and ANVISA in defining the timing of new treatments' availability.

We will closely follow the next steps — pricing, incorporation, and the unfolding of the patent landscape.
