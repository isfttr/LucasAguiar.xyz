7 MIN READ

The Ministry of Health sent a technical opinion to the National Congress in June 2026, with an unfavorable recommendation for PL 5810/2025, which establishes a mechanism for adjusting the term of validity of drug patents in case of delays in the processing of applications at the INPI. The study supporting the opinion was conducted by the **General Coordination for the Promotion and Regulation of the Economic-Industrial Health Complex (CGPR)**, and its numbers are significant: in a moderate scenario, the proposal could generate an additional impact of R$ 3.1 billion for the SUS and R$ 4.5 billion for supplementary healthcare — considering **only four drugs**.

[Lígia Formenti's article, in JOTA](https://www.jota.info/saude/ministerio-da-saude-pl-que-amplia-prazo-de-patente-pode-custar-r-3-bi-a-mais-para-sus), brought the study's details to public attention.

## The Regulatory Context

PL 5810/2025 is part of a debate that gained new momentum after the judgment of **ADI 5.529** by the Supreme Federal Court, which declared the sole paragraph of article 40 of the Industrial Property Law (LPI) unconstitutional. This provision ensured a minimum patent term of ten years from grant — which, in practice, extended the monopoly when the INPI took longer than expected to examine the application. The STF understood that this rule violated the public interest by artificially delaying the entry of competitors into the market.

PL 5810/2025 essentially seeks to recreate this mechanism under a new guise: adjusting the patent term as a way to compensate the applicant for processing delays — an approach internationally known as *Patent Term Adjustment* (PTA).

## The Study Methodology

To project the impact, the CGPR analyzed four high-cost drugs: **Aflibercept**, **Blinatumomab**, **Nivolumab**, and **Ofatumumab**. The choice is not random — these are drugs with patents nearing expiration, indicated for serious conditions (oncological and ophthalmological), and with a consolidated presence in the SUS incorporation lists.

The study considered two scenarios:

| Scenario | Premise | SUS Impact | Supplementary Healthcare Impact |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Conservative** | Current prices maintained during extension | R$ 1.24 billion | R$ 1.81 billion |
| **Moderate** | Reduction of up to 60% (synthetic generics) and 50% (non-new biologics) | R$ 3.11 billion | R$ 4.52 billion |

The document emphasizes that even limited to four drugs, the financial magnitude is already "relevant." Projected for the total universe of drugs with potential for extension, the impact would be substantially greater.

## Systemic Consequences

The CGPR's opinion was not limited to numbers. The document points out that PL 5810/2025, by artificially prolonging monopoly structures:

1.  **Raises prices** — by delaying the entry of generics and biosimilars, it maintains the original manufacturer's price for longer
2.  **Restricts competitive efficiency** — the pharmaceutical market in Brazil already faces significant entry barriers; patent extension adds another obstacle
3.  **Compromises the allocation of public resources** — every additional real spent on drugs under monopoly is a real that is not invested in the incorporation of new technologies, expansion of access, or other health policies

As the technical note states: "resources potentially allocable to the incorporation of new technologies" are diverted to maintain high prices for drugs whose patents should have already expired.

In supplementary healthcare, the effect is also concerning: the document warns that the persistent price increase "tends to pressure healthcare expenditures, compromise the economic-financial sustainability of operators, and intensify cost transfers to beneficiaries."

## Alignment with Industrial Policy

Minister Alexandre Padilha and Vice-President Geraldo Alckmin had already spoken out against the proposal. The CGPR's technical opinion gives analytical depth to this position, with concrete data that allows Congress to evaluate the trade-off between the interests of patent holders and the public interest.

It is worth noting that the Economic-Industrial Health Complex is one of the central axes of **New Brazil Industry (NIB)**, which foresees investments and policies to reduce external dependence on pharmaceutical inputs. Patent extension, in this context, acts in the opposite direction — by prolonging monopolies of foreign manufacturers, it hinders the development of local production capacity.

## What to Expect in Congress

PL 5810/2025 is being processed under priority regime and has strong support from the innovative pharmaceutical industry, which argues that the INPI's delay cannot penalize the applicant. The INPI, in fact, has a history of significant backlog — although it has reduced the stock of pending applications in recent years, as we have already analyzed in [another article]({{< relref "posts/backlog-inpi-patentes-farmaceuticas/" >}}).

The Ministry of Health's opinion, however, puts forward a fact that the Legislative will hardly ignore: the PTA bill is not abstract — it has a value, a recipient (the SUS), and specific drugs. For a Congress that constantly discusses public health funding, the impact of R$ 3.1 billion on just four drugs is a difficult number to circumvent.
