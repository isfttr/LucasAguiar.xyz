7 MIN READ

The Ministry of Health sent a technical opinion to the National Congress in June 2026, recommending against Bill 5810/2025, which establishes a mechanism to adjust the term of validity for drug patents in cases of delays in the processing of applications at the INPI. The study supporting the opinion was conducted by the **General Coordination of Promotion and Regulation of the Health Economic-Industrial Complex (CGPR)**, and its figures are significant: in a moderate scenario, the proposal could generate an additional impact of R$ 3.1 billion for the SUS and R$ 4.5 billion for supplementary health — considering **only four medicines**.

The article by [Lígia Formenti, in JOTA](https://www.jota.info/saude/ministerio-da-saude-pl-que-amplia-prazo-de-patente-pode-custar-r-3-bi-a-mais-para-sus), brought the details of the study to public attention.

## The Regulatory Context

Bill 5810/2025 is part of a debate that gained new momentum after the Supreme Federal Court's judgment of **ADI 5.529**, which declared the sole paragraph of art. 40 of the Industrial Property Law (LPI) unconstitutional. This provision ensured a minimum patent term of ten years from the date of grant — which, in practice, extended the monopoly when the INPI took longer than expected to examine the application. The STF understood that this rule violated public interest by artificially delaying the entry of competitors into the market.

Bill 5810/2025 essentially seeks to recreate this mechanism in a new guise: adjusting the patent term as a way to compensate the applicant for delays in processing — an approach internationally known as *Patent Term Adjustment* (PTA).

## The Study Methodology

To project the impact, the CGPR analyzed four high-cost medicines: **Aflibercept**, **Blinatumomab**, **Nivolumab**, and **Ofatumumab**. The choice is not coincidental — these are drugs with patents nearing expiration, indicated for serious conditions (oncological and ophthalmological), and with a consolidated presence in the SUS incorporation lists.

The study considered two scenarios:

| Scenario | Premise | SUS Impact | Supplementary Health Impact |
|---------|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Conservative** | Current prices maintained during extension | R$ 1.24 billion | R$ 1.81 billion |
| **Moderate** | Reduction of up to 60% (synthetic generics) and 50% (non-new biologics) | R$ 3.11 billion | R$ 4.52 billion |

The document emphasizes that even limited to four medicines, the financial magnitude is already "relevant." Projected for the entire universe of medicines with potential for extension, the impact would be substantially greater.

## Systemic Consequences

The CGPR's opinion was not limited to numbers. The document points out that Bill 5810/2025, by artificially prolonging monopoly structures:

1.  **Raises prices** — by delaying the entry of generics and biosimilars, it maintains the price of the original manufacturer for longer
2.  **Restricts competitive efficiency** — the pharmaceutical market in Brazil already faces significant entry barriers; patent extension adds another obstacle
3.  **Compromises public resource allocation** — every additional real spent on monopolized medicines is a real not invested in the incorporation of new technologies, expansion of access, or other health policies

As the technical note states: "resources potentially allocable to the incorporation of new technologies" are diverted to maintain high prices for medicines whose patents should have already expired.

In supplementary health, the effect is also concerning: the document warns that the persistent rise in prices "tends to pressure healthcare expenditures, compromise the economic-financial sustainability of operators, and intensify cost transfers to beneficiaries."

## Alignment with Industrial Policy

Minister Alexandre Padilha and Vice-President Geraldo Alckmin had already expressed opposition to the proposal. The CGPR's technical opinion provides analytical depth to this position, with concrete data that allows Congress to evaluate the trade-off between the interests of patent holders and the public interest.

It is worth noting that the Health Economic-Industrial Complex is one of the central pillars of the **New Brazil Industry (NIB)**, which foresees investments and policies to reduce external dependence on pharmaceutical inputs. Patent extension, in this context, acts in the opposite direction — by prolonging monopolies of foreign manufacturers, it hinders the development of local productive capacity.

## What to Expect in Congress

Bill 5810/2025 is proceeding under a priority regime and has strong support from the innovative pharmaceutical industry, which argues that INPI's delay cannot penalize the applicant. The INPI, in fact, has a history of significant backlog — although it has reduced the stock of pending applications in recent years, as we have already analyzed in [another article]({{< relref "posts/backlog-inpi-patentes-farmaceuticas/" >}}).

The Ministry of Health's opinion, however, presents a piece of data that the Legislature will hardly ignore: the PTA's cost is not abstract — it has a value, a recipient (the SUS), and specific medicines. For a Congress that constantly discusses public health funding, the R$ 3.1 billion impact on just four medicines is a number difficult to circumvent.
