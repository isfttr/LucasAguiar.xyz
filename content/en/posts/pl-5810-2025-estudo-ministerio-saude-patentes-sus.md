7 MIN READ

In June 2026, the Ministry of Health sent a technical opinion to the National Congress, recommending against PL 5810/2025, which establishes a mechanism to adjust the patent term of medicines in cases of delays in processing applications at INPI. The study supporting the opinion was conducted by the **General Coordination for the Promotion and Regulation of the Health Economic-Industrial Complex (CGPR)**, and its figures are significant: in a moderate scenario, the proposal could generate an additional impact of R$ 3.1 billion for the SUS and R$ 4.5 billion for private health insurance — considering **only four medicines**.

The article by [Lígia Formenti, in JOTA](https://www.jota.info/saude/ministerio-da-saude-pl-que-amplia-prazo-de-patente-pode-custar-r-3-bi-a-mais-para-sus), brought the study's details to public.

## The Regulatory Context

PL 5810/2025 is part of a debate that gained new momentum after the Supreme Federal Court's judgment on **ADI 5.529**, which declared the sole paragraph of art. 40 of the Industrial Property Law (LPI) unconstitutional. This provision ensured a minimum patent term of ten years from the grant date — which, in practice, extended the monopoly when INPI took longer than expected to examine the application. The STF understood that this rule violated public interest by artificially delaying the entry of competitors into the market.

PL 5810/2025 essentially seeks to recreate this mechanism under a new guise: adjusting the patent term as a way to compensate the applicant for processing delays — an approach internationally known as *Patent Term Adjustment* (PTA).

## The Study's Methodology

To project the impact, CGPR analyzed four high-cost medicines: **Aflibercept**, **Blinatumomab**, **Nivolumab**, and **Ofatumumab**. The choice is not casual — these are drugs with patents nearing expiration, indicated for serious conditions (oncological and ophthalmological), and with a consolidated presence in the SUS incorporation lists.

The study considered two scenarios:

| Scenario | Premise | SUS Impact | Private Health Insurance Impact |
|---------|----------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **Conservative** | Current prices maintained during extension | R$ 1.24 billion | R$ 1.81 billion |
| **Moderate** | Reduction of up to 60% (synthetic generics) and 50% (non-new biologics) | R$ 3.11 billion | R$ 4.52 billion |

The document highlights that even limited to four medicines, the financial magnitude is already "relevant". Projected for the total universe of medicines with extension potential, the impact would be substantially greater.

## The Systemic Consequences

CGPR's opinion was not limited to numbers. The document points out that PL 5810/2025, by artificially prolonging monopoly structures:

1.  **Raises prices** — by delaying the entry of generics and biosimilars, it keeps the original manufacturer's price higher for longer.
2.  **Restricts competitive efficiency** — the pharmaceutical market in Brazil already faces significant entry barriers; patent extension adds another obstacle.
3.  **Compromises the allocation of public resources** — every additional real spent on monopolized medicines is a real that is not invested in the incorporation of new technologies, expansion of access, or other health policies.

As the technical note states: "resources potentially directable to the incorporation of new technologies" are diverted to maintain high prices for medicines whose patents should have already expired.

In private health insurance, the effect is also concerning: the document warns that the persistent price increase "tends to pressure healthcare expenses, compromise the economic-financial sustainability of operators, and intensify cost pass-throughs to beneficiaries."

## Alignment with Industrial Policy

Minister Alexandre Padilha and Vice-President Geraldo Alckmin had already spoken out against the proposal. CGPR's technical opinion gives analytical depth to this position, with concrete data that allows Congress to evaluate the trade-off between the interests of patent holders and the public interest.

It is worth noting that the Health Economic-Industrial Complex is one of the central pillars of the **New Brazil Industry (NIB)**, which foresees investments and policies to reduce external dependence on pharmaceutical inputs. Patent extension, in this context, acts in the opposite direction — by prolonging monopolies of foreign manufacturers, it hinders the development of local productive capacity.

## What to Expect in Congress

PL 5810/2025 is being processed under priority regime and has strong support from the innovative pharmaceutical industry, which argues that INPI's delay cannot penalize the applicant. INPI, in fact, has a history of significant backlog — although it has reduced the stock of pending applications in recent years, as we have already analyzed in [another article]({{< relref "posts/backlog-inpi-patentes-farmaceuticas/" >}}).

The Ministry of Health's opinion, however, puts on the table a piece of data that the Legislature will hardly ignore: the cost of PTA is not abstract — it has a value, a recipient (the SUS), and specific medicines. For a Congress that constantly discusses public health funding, the R$ 3.1 billion impact on just four medicines is a difficult number to circumvent.
