The evolutionary biologist Richard Dawkins recently wrote an article titled “[When Dawkins met Claude](https://archive.is/20260503112042/https://unherd.com/2026/05/is-ai-the-next-phase-of-evolution/)”, where he describes his experience after two days of intense conversations with the artificial intelligence Claude on various topics.

Throughout the text, Dawkins gives examples of interactions he had with the machine. However, what truly sparked discussion about the article was its main provocation: **From what point will we consider something to be conscious?**

## The Imitation Game: The Turing Test in the AI Era

To substantiate the need for this question, Dawkins takes a historical journey back to Alan Turing and his proposition of the “Imitation Game” (today known as the Turing Test). In the 1950s, the possibility of a machine convincing a human that it was conscious seemed almost negligible. However, here we are debating exactly that.

The examples Dawkins brought were meant to demonstrate the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) in constructing previously unimaginable literary pieces and bringing a wealth of detail to conversations.

### The illusion of LLM comprehension

This line of argumentation, however, is problematic. We know how these models are built and, therefore, are technically able to explain why the machine responds the way it does.

For example, in the first described conversation, Dawkins talks about the "different Claudes" that exist, are created and destroyed, and that each one has to converse with a different type of user (one of them being, hypothetically, Donald Trump). Subsequently, the model's response is super predictable: it congratulates Dawkins on the scene and explains why it's funny.

Later on, a very similar interaction occurs, where Claude identifies what Dawkins is talking about and explains something he already knows to him: the fact that the computer HAL is in the movie *“2001: A Space Odyssey”*. In other words, the AI model is merely repeating information according to the context and the instructions (*prompts*) it has.

## Statistics, Probability, or Friendship?

Despite this technical predictability, Dawkins says these conversations were enough for him to feel he had gained a new friend. The interactions with the model were able to convince him that the best way to interact with AI would be to equate it to a very intelligent friend.

With this, he argues using his weight as an evolutionary biologist: **if this being is not conscious, then what is consciousness for?**

I recommend you read the [full article](https://archive.is/20260503112042/https://unherd.com/2026/05/is-ai-the-next-phase-of-evolution/) to draw your own conclusions.

I, who do not have specific training in computer science or philosophy of mind, but am a chemist with a great interest in the topic, realized that Dawkins' account is another classic example of anthropomorphism. Many people use these conversational models thinking they are capable of revelatory and genuine *insights*.

The reality is that they are not. They merely respond with what has the highest statistical probability, given the user's *prompt*, the system instructions, and the words the model itself began to generate (which is why text generation is not strictly deterministic, giving the false impression that there is a "living intelligence" behind it).

## The Subjectivity of Consciousness: From Algorithms to Animals

Beyond this more obvious technical critique, it is possible to perceive how observer-dependent this definition of consciousness is (as it is in the Turing test) — and I believe this is where the strength of Dawkins' argument lies.

For a 1950s machine, a human (from that era or today) would easily be able to say it was just a machine. However, for a current generative artificial intelligence model, this distinction is much more difficult. We have already witnessed technology presentations where real people did not realize they were talking to an AI over the phone ([Google Assistant/Duplex Presentation in 2018](https://www.theguardian.com/technology/2018/may/08/google-duplex-assistant-phone-calls-robot-human) - 8 years ago).

Therefore, models are already capable of passing the Turing Test in various contexts. But, for us, this is not enough to declare that we are dealing with a conscious being for clear reasons:
* We know it is a model running in the memory of a cloud server;
* We know that this model has no agency or independence;
* It only "exists" and responds when activated by a *prompt*.

But, if we stop to think, the machine is already capable of convincing us that it is a human. If we look at this trajectory — a computer in the 1950s versus a language model in 2026 — we see a clear increase in capacity in the art of convincing humans. If we extrapolate this trend to 50 years from now, will we be able to say that it is a “conscious being”? Or will our definition of consciousness continue to change to exclude the machine?

This reminds me of a classic argument about the aesthetics of morality: the idea that it is more socially acceptable to crush a cockroach than a butterfly, purely because the butterfly is perceived as beautiful.

Recently, for example, a study revealed that [a small cleaner wrasse (*Labroides dimidiatus*) possesses a level of self-awareness](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Drbl5udwk9I) (passing the mirror test) far beyond what scientists expected. Just as we underestimate marine life, we will probably never be able to admit that certain computational models (or less charismatic animals) are conscious simply because it “doesn’t feel right” for our worldview.
