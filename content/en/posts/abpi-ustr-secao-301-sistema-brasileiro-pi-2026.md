The Brazilian Intellectual Property Association (ABPI) presented on this Monday, July 6, in Washington, a statement to the United States Trade Representative (USTR) in the context of the investigation conducted under Section 301 of the United States Trade Act. The document responds to the preliminary findings released on June 1, 2026, which recommend the imposition of additional 25% tariffs on certain Brazilian products, based, among other aspects, on alleged deficiencies in the Brazilian intellectual property (IP) system.

The Section 301 investigation is one of the most powerful instruments of American trade policy. It allows the USTR to impose unilateral sanctions when it considers that a country's trade practices are unjustified or discriminatory and burden US trade. Brazil has been on the Section 301 Priority Watch List of the Special 301 Report for consecutive years, and this is the first time a formal investigation has advanced to a recommendation of concrete tariff sanctions.

## What the ABPI presented to the USTR

Represented by its vice-president, Rodrigo Ouro Preto, the ABPI structured its statement around three main axes.

**Fight against piracy and counterfeiting.** The association highlighted the coordinated initiatives of the Federal Revenue, the National Council to Combat Piracy (CNCP), the Federal Police, ANATEL, and ANCINE, which have been expanding the inspection of counterfeit goods and combating digital piracy. Brazil has invested in new administrative and regulatory measures to strengthen cooperation among the authorities responsible for IP protection.

**Institutional advances of the INPI.** The ABPI presented to the USTR an overview of the recent achievements of the National Institute of Industrial Property (INPI): reduction in the backlog of pending patent applications, decrease in average examination times, replenishment of the examiner workforce, and implementation of technological tools, including AI-based solutions. These advances were consolidated within the framework of the National Intellectual Property Strategy (ENPI) and the Interministerial Group on Intellectual Property (GIPI), bodies linked to the MDIC.

**Priority examination mechanisms and international cooperation.** The association highlighted the expansion of priority examination programs and cooperation with other patent offices to accelerate the analysis of applications, including in the biopharmaceutical sector — an area of particular interest to American industry.

## The context of the INPI's achievements

The ABPI's statement to the USTR does not occur in a vacuum. The INPI has been accumulating concrete deliveries that strengthen Brazil's position in bilateral negotiations.

In June 2026, the Federal Regional Court of the 2nd Region (TRF-2) unanimously upheld a decision requiring the Union to guarantee structural conditions for the proper functioning of the INPI, in a lawsuit filed by ABPI itself — a judicial recognition that the institute needs continuous investment to fulfill its mission. See more about this decision in the post [TRF-2 Orders Union to Restructure INPI]({{< relref "posts/trf2-decisao-abpi-fortalecimento-inpi/" >}}).

The INPI also published, in 2025, its ambitious automation plan for the period 2025-2029, with investments in artificial intelligence, process digitization, and modernization of patent examination systems. This modernization roadmap, detailed in [INPI 4.0: The INPI Automation Plan (2025-2029)]({{< relref "posts/inpi-automation-roadmap-2025-2029/" >}}), provides for reducing the patent backlog and improving examination times — exactly the points raised by the ABPI in Washington.

More recently, in July 2026, the INPI launched a new version of the Patent Services Module, unifying the filing of applications, payment of annuities, and gradually eliminating the old Electronic Petitioning system. The changes are detailed in [INPI launches new version of the Patent Module]({{< relref "posts/inpi-modulo-servicos-patentes-novas-funcionalidades-2026/" >}}).

## What is at stake

The 25% tariffs recommended by the USTR, if implemented, would have a direct impact on Brazilian exports to the United States — one of Brazil's main trading partners. Although the USTR based its recommendation on multiple grounds, intellectual property occupies a central position in the argument.

For the Brazilian IP community, the outcome of this investigation could have long-term consequences:

- **Regulatory pressure.** An unfavorable determination could lead the US to demand legislative or regulatory changes in the Brazilian IP system as a condition for removing the tariffs, similar to what happened with Ukraine and Argentina in past Section 301 investigations.

- **Signal to investors.** The international perception of Brazil's IP environment influences investment decisions in R&D, technology transfer, and the establishment of innovation centers in the country.

- **Precedent for other sectors.** If IP is used as a justification for trade sanctions, other sectors of the Brazilian economy may be subject to similar investigations.

## The ABPI's central argument

The ABPI argued that the conclusions of the USTR's preliminary report "do not adequately reflect the current reality of the country" and that any concerns related to IP protection should be addressed through technical dialogue, bilateral cooperation, and evidence-based assessments — instruments that have historically contributed to strengthening economic and trade relations between Brazil and the United States.

The association reaffirmed that Brazil ensures equal treatment for domestic and foreign holders of IP rights, both in the administrative sphere and in the Judiciary, and that the country has been adopting concrete and continuous measures to improve the system, coordinated within the ENPI and GIPI frameworks.

## Next steps

The Section 301 process is ongoing. After the public comment phase, of which the ABPI's statement is a part, the USTR will issue a final determination, which may confirm, modify, or archive the tariff recommendation. The exact timeline depends on the administrative process and any bilateral negotiations between the Brazilian and American governments.

The ABPI's participation in the process — presenting concrete data and a technical and institutional vision — is a relevant step to demonstrate that the Brazilian IP system is evolving, with measurable advances that deserve international recognition.

## What to watch

- The formal response of the Brazilian government to the Section 301 investigation
- Possible diplomatic efforts by MDIC and Itamaraty with the USTR
- New data from INPI on backlog reduction and examination times — concrete indicators that may influence the final determination
- The progress of discussions on IP within the GIPI and ENPI, which may generate new measures to strengthen the system
