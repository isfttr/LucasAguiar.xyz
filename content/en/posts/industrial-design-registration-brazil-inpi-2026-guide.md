Industrial design registration protects the ornamental appearance of an object — the three-dimensional shape of a package, a piece of furniture, a toy, or two-dimensional aspects like fabric patterns or decorative motifs on ceramics. Unlike patents (which protect technical function) or trademarks (which protect commercial identity), industrial design protects the **visual design** of a product.

In Brazil, design rights are granted by the National Institute of Industrial Property (INPI) and regulated by the Industrial Property Law (Law 9,279/96), articles 95-106. This guide covers the entire process: what can and cannot be registered, costs, documents, step-by-step electronic filing, validity periods, and the international route through the Hague Agreement.

> **Updated July 2026:** INPI published its latest Industrial Design Manual update on June 26, 2026, with changes to document copies, union priority proof, and compliance petitions. The e-DI system remains in normal operation.

## What is an Industrial Design?

**Article 95 of the Brazilian IP Law** defines industrial design as "the ornamental plastic form of an object or the ornamental set of lines and colors that can be applied to a product, providing a new and original visual result in its external configuration and capable of serving as a type of industrial manufacture."

In practical terms, industrial design covers:

| What can be registered | Examples |
|----------------------|----------|
| **Three-dimensional shape** | Design of a bottle, chair, smartphone, jewelry, lamp |
| **Two-dimensional pattern** | Fabric print, wallpaper pattern, graphic applied to a surface |
| **Ornamental set** | Shape + pattern combination on a single product |
| **Part of a product** | Bag handle, appliance base (as long as visible and ornamental) |

**What CANNOT be registered as industrial design:**

- Pure artistic works (sculptures, paintings — protected by copyright)
- Technical or functional aspects (protected by invention patents or utility models)
- Distinctive signs (protected as trademarks)
- Engineering works or architectural projects
- Shapes solely determined by technical function

> ⚠️ **Important:** if a product's shape is determined exclusively by its technical function, it **cannot** be registered as an industrial design. This prevents "evergreening" of functional designs.

## Industrial Design vs. Other IP Protections

| Aspect | Industrial Design | Invention Patent | Utility Model | Trademark |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------|---------------|-----------|
| **Protects** | Ornamental appearance | New inventive invention | Functional improvement | Commercial identity |
| **Substantive exam** | Yes (novelty + originality) | Yes (inventive step) | Yes (inventive step) | Yes (distinctiveness) |
| **Validity term** | 10 yrs + 3 × 5 yr renewals = **25 yrs** | 20 years | 15 years | 10 yrs (renewable indefinitely) |
| **Novelty standard** | Worldwide (prior art) | Worldwide | Worldwide | National (conflict check) |
| **Average grant time** | 6-12 months | 5-10 years | 3-5 years | 12-18 months |
| **Publication** | Automatic (18 months) | Automatic (18 months) | Automatic (18 months) | After formal exam |

Industrial design is generally the **fastest and cheapest** route to protect a manufactured product's appearance.

## Legal Requirements: Novelty and Originality

Two requirements must be met:

**1. Novelty (Art. 96):** the design must not be part of the prior art — everything made accessible to the public before the filing date, anywhere in the world, through written or oral description, use, or any other means.

**2. Originality (Art. 97):** the design must result in a distinctive visual configuration compared to prior objects. Inventive step is not required (as in patents), but the visual result must not be trivial or an obvious combination of known elements.

### Grace Period (Art. 96, §2)

The creator can disclose the design **up to 180 days before filing** without losing novelty, provided the disclosure was made by the creator, by third parties based on the creator's information, or by INPI in official publication. This is important for trade fairs, exhibitions, and commercial presentations.

## Required Documents

To file an industrial design application at INPI:

1. **Reproductions of the design** — figures or photographs showing the object from all necessary angles (perspective, front, side, top, bottom). The Design Manual (section 5.5) details presentation rules:
   - Format: JPEG or PNG
   - Minimum resolution: 300 DPI
   - Neutral background (preferably white)
   - Hatching lines for transparency or materials
2. **Descriptive report** (optional but recommended) — describes what each view represents
3. **Power of attorney** — if filed by an attorney/agent
4. **GRU payment receipt** — with the number generated in the e-INPI system
5. **Priority declaration** — if claiming union priority (6-month deadline from foreign filing)

> **3D vs. 2D designs:** INPI treats them differently. 3D designs require multiple views (typically 6-7: perspective, front, back, left side, right side, top, bottom). 2D designs (patterns) may require only a single front view with rapport indication when applicable.

## Step-by-Step Registration

### Step 1: Register on e-INPI

Create an account on [e-INPI](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/e-inpi), INPI's electronic services portal. Individuals use their gov.br account (silver or gold level). Legal entities need an ICP-Brasil digital certificate from their legal representative.

### Step 2: Generate and pay the GRU

Access the [GRU Issuance System](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/desenhos-industriais/custos-e-pagamento) and issue the federal collection form for the desired fee code (filing = 300, examination = 301, etc.).

- Payment via barcode at any Brazilian bank
- Save the GRU number — required for filing
- The GRU expires in 30 days

### Step 3: File the application on e-DI

After paying the GRU, access the [e-DI system](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/desenhos-industriais) and:

1. Select "New Industrial Design Application"
2. Enter the paid GRU number
3. Fill in applicant and creator details (can be different persons)
4. Upload design figures/photographs
5. If claiming priority, attach the priority document
6. Confirm and submit

### Step 4: Formal examination

INPI checks whether documentation is complete. If issues arise, an **office action** is published in the IP Gazette (RPI) — you have 60 days (extendable by another 60) to respond.

### Step 5: Substantive examination

INPI examines novelty and originality against worldwide prior art. If approved, the registration is granted and published in the RPI.

### Step 6: Certificate

After approval and payment of the certificate fee (code 310), INPI issues the registration certificate.

**Estimated total time:** 6 to 18 months — significantly faster than patents (which can take 5-10 years).

## Validity Term and Maintenance

Industrial design registration has an initial term of **10 years** from the filing date, renewable for **three successive 5-year periods**, totaling up to **25 years** of protection.

| Period | Action | Fee |
|--------|--------|-----|
| Years 1-5 | Initial maintenance fee (included in filing) | — |
| Year 5 | 5-year maintenance | Code 305 |
| Year 10 | 1st renewal (5 years) | Code 305 |
| Year 15 | 2nd renewal (5 years) | Code 305 |
| Year 20 | 3rd renewal (5 years) | Code 305 |

> ⚠️ **The maintenance fee (code 305) must be paid every 5 years from the filing date.** Non-payment results in registration expiry. INPI sends reminders through the "My Applications" system.

## Hague Agreement: International Registration

Brazil acceded to the **Hague Agreement for the International Registration of Industrial Designs** in 2023, administered by WIPO. A single international application can protect a design in up to **96 countries**.

### Advantages of the Hague System

- **Single application** in one language (English, French, or Spanish)
- **Unified fees** in Swiss francs (CHF)
- Up to **100 designs** per application (same Locarno class)
- **Minimum 15 years** of international protection (renewable up to each country's maximum)
- In Brazil, the term via Hague also reaches **25 years**

### How to file via Hague

1. Access [eHague](https://www.wipo.int/hague/en/eHague/) — WIPO's online platform
2. Fill the form and upload reproductions
3. Designate the countries where protection is sought (include Brazil for national protection)
4. Pay fees: basic + publication + designation per country
5. WIPO examines formal requirements and publishes in the International Designs Bulletin

Each designated country may conduct its own substantive examination — Brazil's INPI has 6 months to refuse protection based on national legislation.

## What Changed in the INPI Design Manual (June 2026)

On June 26, 2026, INPI published its latest Design Manual update. Key changes:

1. **Document copies:** clearer guidelines on how to request document copies from DI processes
2. **Union priority obtained by assignment:** new documentary requirements when priority rights are acquired via assignment from a third party
3. **Compliance petitions:** adjustments to the response filing process for office actions

The update is targeted but important for those working with union priority (foreign filing followed by Brazilian filing within 6 months).

## Conclusion

Industrial design registration is a strategic and underutilized tool in Brazil. With far shorter grant times than patents, accessible fees (especially with 70% discounts for individuals and SMEs), and international protection via the Hague Agreement, design registration should be on the radar of any company investing in product design.

The June 2026 Manual update shows INPI is fine-tuning procedures to keep pace with growing application volumes — driven by Brazil's 2023 Hague Agreement accession and increasing awareness of industrial design as a valuable IP asset.
