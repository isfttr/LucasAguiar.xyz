Brazil's patent office (INPI) has published the 2026 edition of **"Brazil in the Global Intangible Investment Landscape,"** produced in partnership with the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) and Luiss Business School. The report arrives on the same day WIPO announced that global intangible investment — software, data, brands, R&D, organizational capital — **surpassed USD 10 trillion for the first time in 2025.**

The two documents, which complement each other, offer the most complete picture ever produced of how intangible value is being generated and measured in Brazil and worldwide.

## The Global Picture: USD 10 Trillion and Accelerating Growth

The third edition of the **World Intangible Investment Highlights** (WIPO/LBS, 2026) covers 29 high- and middle-income economies, accounting for roughly 57% of global GDP. The numbers are striking:

| Indicator | Value | Period |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| Global intangible investment | **USD 10 trillion** | 2025 |
| Annual growth (intangibles) | **5.5%** | 2020–2025 |
| Annual growth (tangibles) | **3.2%** | 2020–2025 |
| Share of GDP in covered economies | **~13%** | 2025 |
| US investment | **~USD 5 trillion** | 2025 |
| Japan | **USD 810 billion** | 2024 |
| Germany | **USD 695 billion** | 2024 |

The United States accounts for nearly half of the global total. The gap between the US and the next four largest economies combined has roughly doubled over the past decade, from about USD 1 trillion (2015) to USD 2 trillion (2025). Japan rose to second place globally with 4.8% growth in intangible investment in 2024, even as its tangible investment stagnates.

## Brazil in the Ranking: 7th Largest Intangible Economy

The INPI report places Brazil as the **seventh-largest economy worldwide in absolute intangible investment**, with USD 312 billion invested in 2023:

- Close to Canada, just below Italy
- Ahead of Spain, the Netherlands, Sweden, and India
- Equivalent to **7.6% of Brazil's GDP**
- Comparable scale to agriculture (6.0% of GDP) and larger than extractive industries including mining and oil (3.7%)

In **brands**, Brazil is the **fifth-largest economy globally** in absolute investment (USD 79 billion), surpassing France, Italy, and Spain. Brands account for 25% of Brazil's intangible investment — a higher share than in the United Kingdom.

## The Measurement Gap: 72% Missing from National Accounts

One of the most significant findings in the INPI report is that **roughly 72% of Brazil's intangible investment is not captured by National Accounts**. This is not an error — it reflects an international accounting convention that treats spending on brands, organizational capital, and training as current expenses rather than investment.

The same underestimation pattern appears, to varying degrees, across all 29 economies covered. The practical consequence is that GDP and productivity in intangible-intensive economies — which Brazil already is — may be **systematically understated**.

The **Global INTAN-Invest Database**, maintained by the WIPO-Luiss Business School partnership, aims to fill this gap by following the national accounts framework proposed by Corrado, Hulten and Sichel (2005, 2009), covering both measured and unmeasured intangible assets.

## AI's Two Investment Waves

The WIPO report highlights how artificial intelligence is accelerating investment through two distinct waves:

**1. Tangible infrastructure** — data centers, semiconductors, power systems and networks. This wave is strong but geographically concentrated (primarily in the US).

**2. Intangible assets** — data, software, R&D, brands, organizational capital and training. The report argues that AI's lasting economic impact will come less from physical infrastructure and more from the intangible assets built on top of it.

As Cecilia Jona-Lasinio, Professor at Luiss Business School and co-author of the report, notes: *"AI is not just a new technology. It is changing how knowledge itself is produced, making intangible investment the key to understanding its economic impact."*

For Brazil, which already invests USD 312 billion in intangibles, the question is how to accelerate the second wave — data, software, and organizational innovation — rather than relying solely on imported infrastructure.

## Implications for IP Policy

The INPI report's findings have direct implications for Brazilian IP policy:

- **Brands as strategic assets**: Brazil is already the 5th largest brand investor globally. Recent moves — the public consultation on position marks and accession to the Madrid Protocol — suggest INPI is preparing for a more sophisticated brand ecosystem.
- **Accounting blind spot**: if 72% of intangible investment is invisible to national accounts, industrial policy decisions based solely on traditional GDP may be underestimating innovation's real weight in the economy.
- **Open innovation profile**: the fact that brands represent 25% of Brazil's intangible investment (above the UK) suggests a market-oriented innovation profile, not just traditional R&D.
- **WIPO data as a compass**: Brazil now appears in WIPO reports across PCT patents, international trademarks, and intangible investment — three metrics telling the same story of a maturing IP ecosystem.

## What to Watch

The full INPI report "Brazil in the Global Intangible Investment Landscape" is available on the INPI website, and the complete **World Intangible Investment Highlights 2026** can be accessed on WIPO's portal. Both documents are expected to be updated annually.

For anyone working in IP, innovation, and industrial policy, this is a milestone: for the first time, Brazil has a systematic diagnosis of its intangible investment, aligned with international methodology. The next step is to see how this diagnosis translates into policy — in the LPI reform, INPI's strategic planning, and Brazil's industrial strategy.
