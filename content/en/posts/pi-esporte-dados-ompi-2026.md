On April 26, 2026, the World Intellectual Property Organization (OMPI/WIPO) released an unprecedented report as part of the World Intellectual Property Day celebrations. With the theme "IP and Sports: Ready, Set, Innovate", the report shows that the sports industry is undergoing a silent transformation — and intellectual property is at its core.

The numbers are impressive: between 2016 and 2025, over **65,700 inventions** related to sports were registered, accompanied by **1.25 million trademarks** and **70,000 industrial designs**. But the most revealing data is the growth rate: across all types of IP, sports are growing well above the global average.

## Patents in Sports: 7.6% Growth Per Year

Over a decade (2016–2025), sports-related patents grew **7.6% per year**, compared to 4.4% general growth for all patent applications. This means that sports are innovating at a pace **73% faster** than the industry average.

| Indicator | Sports | Global Average | Difference |
|-----------|---------|-------------|-----------|
| Patents (annual growth) | 7.6% | 4.4% | +73% |
| Trademarks (annual growth) | 6.1% | ~3.3% | +85% |
| Industrial designs (annual growth) | 8.3% | ~4.5% | +84% |

*Source: WIPO Technology SPARK Report, 2026.*

## Which Sports Lead in Innovation?

Not all sports are equal when it comes to patenting. The report indicates that **golf, swimming, and racket sports** (tennis, badminton, squash) are the most patent-intensive disciplines. The reason is simple: these are sports where equipment — clubs, rackets, balls, swimwear — is in constant technological evolution.

In the field of **industrial designs**, fitness and gymnastics technologies lead. This reflects the importance of design and user experience in products aimed at the end consumer. The largest design applicants in 2025 were Srixon, Decathlon, and Callaway.

## Trademarks: Where Disney and Apple Compete with NBA and WWE

One of the most interesting findings of the report is the list of the largest owners of sports-related trademarks. Alongside traditional names like NBA, WWE, and Decathlon, technology and entertainment giants such as **Disney, Apple, and Amazon** appear.

This reveals the multifaceted nature of IP in modern sports:
- **Disney** holds trademarks linked to sports broadcasts (ESPN) and thematic content
- **Apple** protects wearable technologies (Apple Watch) and sports streaming services
- **Amazon** registers trademarks related to broadcasts (Prime Video sports) and event logistics

## The Geography of Sports Innovation

Asia dominates the filing of patents (63%) and industrial designs (76%) linked to sports, reflecting the industrial strength of countries like China, Japan, and South Korea in the manufacturing of sports equipment. North America accounts for 22% of patents, while Europe stands out in trademark activities (43%), driven by the density of European sports clubs and leagues.

For low and middle-income countries, including Brazil, the most relevant data is the **double-digit growth** in sports IP activity. The report suggests that sports can serve as an accessible entry point for innovation and IP awareness in developing economies.

## What Comes After the Numbers

Beyond the data, the WIPO report points out that technologies such as **wearable devices, data analytics, smart equipment, and refereeing technologies** are reshaping sports in practice — improving performance, fairness, and fan engagement.

Many of these innovations are overflowing into areas such as health, entertainment, and consumer products, creating a multiplier effect that goes far beyond stadiums.

To coordinate these efforts, WIPO has created a **specific workstream on IP and Sports** and will intensify assistance to athletes, sports associations, and Member States. The mandate also includes **e-sports**, a rapidly growing area that raises its own intellectual property issues.

## What This Means for Brazil

Although Brazil does not appear among the largest filers — which is expected, given that the country accounts for less than 1% of global PCT applications — the report's data offers three important lessons:

1. **Opportunity in sports innovation:** With upcoming events such as the 2026 World Cup (USA, Canada, Mexico) and the Los Angeles 2028 Olympic Games, Brazilian companies in sports equipment, apparel, and technology can use the IP system to protect innovations and seek international markets.

2. **IP growth in developing countries** — the report shows double-digit growth in low and middle-income economies. Brazil can benefit from this movement, especially in the fitness, aquatic sports (swimming is one of the most patent-intensive sports), and football sectors.

3. **The interface between digital IP and sports** — wearables, data analytics, streaming, and e-sports are areas where Brazil has active startups and developers. WIPO's new workstream on IP and Sports can be a channel for the country to contribute to the global agenda.

## Conclusion

WIPO's "Technology SPARK" report confirms what many IP professionals already intuited: sports have ceased to be merely a stage for athletic performance and have become an innovation laboratory intensive in intellectual property. With growth rates far exceeding the average in patents, trademarks, and designs, the sports sector represents a strategic frontier for global IP — and Brazil, as a sports powerhouse and emerging economy, has everything to be part of this story.
