Danish pharmaceutical company Novo Nordisk filed a lawsuit against EMS — the largest Brazilian pharmaceutical company — requesting the annulment of the **Ozivy** brand registration, the first 100% national semaglutide-based weight-loss pen approved in Brazil. INPI also appears as a defendant in the lawsuit, filed at the 31st Federal Court of Rio de Janeiro.

The case, revealed by [Pharmaceutical Panorama](https://panoramafarmaceutico.com.br/novo-nordisk-pede-anulacao-da-marca-ozivy/) on June 22, 2026, exposes the tenuous boundaries between legitimate competition and undue exploitation of reputation in the billion-dollar GLP-1 drug market.

## What Novo Nordisk alleges

Novo Nordisk's central argument is that EMS **violated the Ozempic and Wegovy brands** by naming its product. According to the company's lawyers, the name Ozivy:

- **Creates a risk of confusion** among consumers, who may erroneously associate EMS's product with Novo Nordisk
- **Represents "parasitic exploitation"** (*aproveitamento parasitário*) of the reputation built by the original brands over years of investment in marketing and positioning
- **Could have been different** — EMS could have adopted any other name, but chose to create a name that starts with "OZ" (like Ozempic) and ends with "VY" (like Wegovy)

The argument of "parasitic exploitation" (also known as *free riding* or *unfair advantage*) is provided for in the Industrial Property Law (Law 9.279/96) and is one of the grounds for protecting well-known trademarks. Brands like Ozempic and Wegovy, which have become household names far beyond the medical community, enjoy special protection in Brazil.

## EMS's defense

EMS, in turn, received the lawsuit "with tranquility," according to an official statement. The company maintains that:

- The **Ozivy brand is original** and was developed through a **technical and independent pharmaceutical branding process**
- Trusts in the maintenance of the registration granted by INPI
- Will continue to focus on expanding Brazilian patients' access to quality treatments

The national pharmaceutical company also defends free competition and respect for the country's regulatory and institutional decisions.

## What Brazilian law says

Law 9.279/96 (LPI) establishes, in its article 124, that the following cannot be registered as trademarks:

> *"Reproduction or imitation of a characteristic or differentiating element of a trade name or company name of third parties, liable to cause confusion or association with these distinctive signs."* (sub-item XIX)

It also prohibits the registration of a trademark that:

> *"Reproduces or imitates, in whole or in part, a third party's registered trademark to distinguish or identify an identical, similar or related product or service, liable to cause confusion or association."* (sub-item XIX, first part)

INPI, when registering Ozivy, considered that there was no conflict with Novo Nordisk's prior trademarks. Now it will be up to the Judiciary to review this decision.

| Element | Ozempic | Wegovy | Ozivy |
|----------|---------|--------|-------|
| Owner | Novo Nordisk | Novo Nordisk | EMS |
| Indication | Type 2 Diabetes | Obesity | Obesity/Weight loss |
| Substance | Semaglutide | Semaglutide | Semaglutide |
| INPI Registration | Granted | Granted | Granted (contested) |
| Prefix | **OZ** | **WE** | **OZ** |
| Suffix | **empic** | **govy** | **ivy** |

The table above illustrates the core of the discussion: while the "OZ-" beginning echoes Ozempic, the "-VY" ending refers to Wegovy. For the Judiciary, the question will be to determine if these similarities are sufficient to generate confusion in the average consumer of medicines.

## What's at stake

The GLP-1 market in Brazil is expected to reach **US$ 9 billion by 2030**, according to sectoral projections. Semaglutide — the active ingredient in Ozempic, Wegovy, and now Ozivy — is at the center of this market.

- **Ozempic** (semaglutide 1 mg) is approved for type 2 diabetes and has significant off-label use for weight loss
- **Wegovy** (semaglutide 2.4 mg) is the version specifically approved for obesity
- **Ozivy** (EMS) is the first national version, with the potential to significantly reduce prices in the Brazilian market

The outcome of the lawsuit could have implications that go far beyond the involved parties:

1.  **For EMS**: losing the trademark registration would mean having to rename the product, with rebranding costs and loss of marketing investment already made at launch
2.  **For INPI**: an unfavorable decision regarding its examination could question the consistency of trademark conflict analyses in the pharmaceutical area
3.  **For the market**: sets precedents regarding the level of protection for well-known trademarks in the Brazilian pharmaceutical sector
4.  **For consumers**: a potential rebranding of Ozivy could create temporary confusion in the market, precisely what the lawsuit seeks to prevent

## Lessons for the IP and innovation ecosystem

This case illustrates why **pharmaceutical branding** is an area of extremely high sensitivity in Industrial Property. Unlike other sectors, where brands can coexist with moderate similarities, in the pharmaceutical sector, the risk of confusion involves not only purchasing decisions but also public health issues — a patient who confuses medications can suffer serious consequences.

For companies developing products similar to consolidated brands, the lesson is clear: the naming strategy must undergo **rigorous trademark due diligence**, preferably including an anteriority search at INPI and a collision risk analysis with well-known brands. The temptation to create names that "echo" market-leading brands can result in costly litigation and the risk of losing all launch investment.

For IP firms and legal departments, the case reinforces the importance of actively monitoring competitors' trademark registrations and acting quickly — which Novo Nordisk did by challenging Ozivy practically in the week of its launch.
