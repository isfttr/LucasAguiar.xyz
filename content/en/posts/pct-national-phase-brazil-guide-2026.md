In 2025, international patent applications filed through WIPO's Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) rose 0.7% to 275,900 filings worldwide. Digital communication (+6.1%) and semiconductors (+6.1%) recorded the fastest growth rates among the top ten technology fields, according to WIPO data released in March 2026.

For inventors and companies who file a PCT application and want to protect their invention in Brazil, the next step is **national phase entry** — converting the international application into a Brazilian patent application before the National Institute of Industrial Property (INPI). This guide covers everything you need to know: deadlines, documents, fees, procedures, and common pitfalls.

## What is the PCT and why does the national phase exist

The Patent Cooperation Treaty (PCT) is an international treaty administered by WIPO that allows filing a single international patent application with effect in over 150 contracting countries. It does not grant patents — national offices (such as INPI) do that, after national phase entry.

The system has two phases:

| Phase | Description | Deadline |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **International phase** | PCT filing, international search, publication (18 months), preliminary examination (optional) | 0 to 30 months from priority |
| **National phase** | Conversion of the PCT application into a national application in each country where protection is sought | Up to 30 months from priority (standard) |

Brazil has been a PCT signatory since 1978 (Decree No. 81,742/1978), and national phase entry is regulated by Articles 21 to 27 of the Brazilian Industrial Property Law (Law 9,279/96) and INPI Resolution No. 294/2022.

## Deadline: 30 months from priority

The standard deadline for entering the national phase in Brazil is **30 months from the international filing date** (earliest priority date, if any). This is the deadline established by PCT Article 22(1), adopted by the INPI.

**Important:** The deadline is non-extendable. If the 30-month deadline is missed, the PCT application loses effect in Brazil. There is no restoration mechanism — except for cases of force majeure duly proven (PCT Article 22(3)).

For PCT applications claiming Convention priority (Art. 4 of the Paris Convention), count 30 months from the earliest priority date, not the PCT filing date.

## Required documents for national phase entry

When entering the national phase at INPI, the applicant must submit:

1. **National phase entry petition** — electronic form in the e-Patentes or e-PCT system
2. **Description, claims, drawings, and abstract** — translated into Portuguese, except where specific resolutions provide exceptions
3. **Power of attorney** — if represented by an agent (not required for initial filing, but necessary for subsequent acts such as responding to office actions)
4. **Proof of payment of the basic fee** (GRU) — specific code for national phase entry
5. **Proof of priority document fee** — when applicable (if claiming Convention priority)
6. **Assignment document** — if the PCT applicant is not the inventor and ownership needs to be proven

### Translation requirement

The entire PCT application content (description, claims, drawings with text, abstract) must be filed in Portuguese. The translation can be done by a sworn translator or public translator — INPI does not require specific certification, but a qualified professional is recommended to avoid errors that could compromise the scope of protection.

## Step-by-step national phase entry

### 1. Check the deadline

Confirm the earliest priority date of your PCT application and calculate the 30-month limit. Start the process **at least 60 days in advance** to avoid issues with translation, document gathering, and payments.

### 2. Prepare the translation

Translate into Brazilian Portuguese:
- Specification (description)
- Claims
- Abstract
- Text in drawings
- Any other textual documents accompanying the application

### 3. Access the INPI e-PCT system

Since 2025, INPI provides the e-PCT module integrated with the e-Patentes system. Access is via [gov.br](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/servicos/patentes), using a gov.br account with silver or gold level.

### 4. Fill out the entry form

In the system, select "National Phase Entry of PCT Application." Provide:
- International application number (PCT/XX2025/XXXXXX)
- International filing date
- Applicant and inventor names
- Claimed priorities (if any)
- Title of the invention in Portuguese

### 5. Attach documents

Attach files in INPI-accepted formats (PDF/A for text documents, XML for structured data when applicable).

### 6. Generate and pay the GRU

The GRU (Federal Collection Guide) is generated within the INPI system. The specific fee code for PCT national phase entry is **200** (patent filing) or the specific PCT code, according to the current fee table.

### 7. File the application

After payment, the system generates an INPI process number (BR 10 2026 XXXXXXX-X). Keep this number — it is the identifier for your Brazilian patent application.

## What happens after entry

Once the national phase entry is filed, the application follows the standard Brazilian patent prosecution process:

1. **Publication** — 18 months after filing (or earliest priority), the application is published in the Revista da Propriedade Industrial (RPI)
2. **Examination request** — must be filed within 36 months of the filing date (Art. 33 LPI). Without an examination request, the application is shelved
3. **Technical examination** — INPI analyzes patentability: novelty, inventive step, and industrial applicability (Arts. 8-15 LPI)
4. **Office actions** — if the examiner raises issues, the applicant has 60 or 90 days to respond
5. **Grant or rejection** — after examination, INPI either grants or rejects the patent
6. **Annuities** — annual payment required from the 3rd year onward (Art. 84 LPI), under penalty of forfeiture

## Common pitfalls (and how to avoid them)

### Missed deadline

The leading cause of rights loss in Brazil for PCT applications is missing the 30-month deadline. INPI **does not accept late national phase entry petitions**. Active monitoring and a deadline calendar from the PCT filing date are recommended.

### Inaccurate translation

Translation errors in claims can reduce or alter the scope of protection. Unlike some countries, INPI does not accept significant corrections after filing — the translation presented at national phase entry is binding.

### Missing examination request

The examination request must be filed within 36 months of filing. Many applicants miss this deadline by failing to monitor their case. Unlike the 30-month entry deadline, the examination deadline can sometimes be reinstated, but at additional cost and risk.

### Annuity payment

Annuities begin counting from the 3rd year of filing but are due even during examination. Non-payment for more than 6 months leads to definitive shelving.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do I need a local agent in Brazil for national phase entry?**
Not mandatory for the initial petition, but highly recommended. A qualified agent can receive notifications, respond to office actions, and perform all procedural acts. INPI accepts powers of attorney in Portuguese or English.

**Can I enter the national phase before 30 months?**
Yes. Early entry is permitted and can accelerate examination. Simply submit complete documentation and pay the fees before the deadline.

**Does INPI accept PCT applications in English?**
No. Documentation must be in Portuguese. The translation can be submitted at entry or, in exceptional cases, upon commitment to submit later (with additional costs).

**What if I only want protection in Brazil, without PCT?**
In this case, a direct patent filing at INPI (national application via Paris Convention) is simpler and cheaper. PCT is advantageous when seeking protection in multiple countries.

**Does the priority examination program apply to PCT applications?**
Yes. PCT national phase applications can request priority examination (INPI Resolution No. 283/2021) if they fall within eligible categories such as green technologies, public health, or specific ICT areas. In July 2026, INPI resumed priority examination for H04 (Electrical Communication) patents.
