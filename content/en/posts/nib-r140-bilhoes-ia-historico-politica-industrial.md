The National Bank for Economic and Social Development (BNDES) and the Financier of Studies and Projects (Finep) announced on June 22, 2026, the availability of an additional R$ 140 billion to finance projects under the New Brazil Industry (NIB) until December 2026. With the new contribution, the total volume of resources mobilized by the industrial policy reaches over R$ 750 billion in the period from 2023 to 2026.

The announcement was made during the ceremony commemorating BNDES's 74th anniversary, in Rio de Janeiro, with the presence of President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva and Vice President and Minister of Development, Industry, Commerce, and Services, Geraldo Alckmin. Of the total amount, R$ 102.5 billion will be made available by BNDES and R$ 37.5 billion by Finep.

The most significant novelty, however, is the inclusion of artificial intelligence (AI) among the strategic sectors of industrial policy — alongside fertilizers, agricultural machinery, active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs), biopharmaceuticals, advanced therapies, sustainable mobility, audiovisual, critical minerals, and dual-use technologies.

This article reconstructs the trajectory of more than two decades of industrial policy in Brazil to show that this decision was not made overnight.

## The long road to the New Brazil Industry

### 2004 — PITCE: the first industrial policy of the 21st century

The Industrial, Technological, and Foreign Trade Policy (PITCE) was Brazil's first systemic attempt to articulate an industrial development agenda focused on innovation. Created by Law No. 11,080/2004, PITCE established the National Council for Industrial Development (CNDI) as a coordination body between the government and the productive sector ([CNDI on the MDIC website](https://www.gov.br/mdic/pt-br/composicao/se/cndi)).

### 2008 — PDP: scale and priority sectors

The Productive Development Policy (PDP) succeeded PITCE with more ambitious goals and a sectoral focus: pharmaceuticals, defense, ICT, and renewable energy. It was the first Brazilian industrial policy to explicitly include information technologies as a strategic vector ([MDIC — History of Industrial Policy](https://www.gov.br/mdic/pt-br/assuntos/politica-industrial)).

### 2011 — Greater Brazil Plan

Launched with the motto "Innovate to Compete. Compete to Grow," the Greater Brazil Plan (PBM) brought payroll tax exemptions, tax incentives for innovation, and ambitious goals — to increase fixed capital investments from 18.4% to 23% of GDP ([Wikipedia — Greater Brazil Plan](https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plano_Brasil_Maior)).

### 2016–2022 — the institutional vacuum

The CNDI was deactivated during the Temer government and not reactivated during the Bolsonaro government. For six years, Brazil operated without a coordinated industrial policy. The manufacturing industry's share of GDP persistently shrank ([Transition Report 2022](https://static.poder360.com.br/2022/12/Relatorio-final-da-transicao-de-Lula.pdf)).

### April 2023 — recreation of the CNDI

Decree No. 11,482 recreated the CNDI with the mission of proposing guidelines for the new industrial policy. Chaired by Vice President Geraldo Alckmin, the council brought together 21 ministries, representatives from industry, academia, and workers.

### January 2024 — launch of the New Brazil Industry

On January 22, 2024, the government launched the NIB with R$ 300 billion in financing planned until 2026 and six missions: (1) sustainable agro-industrial chains, (2) healthcare complex, (3) infrastructure and mobility, (4) digital transformation, (5) bioeconomy, and (6) defense ([Agência Brasil](https://agenciabrasil.ebc.com.br/economia/noticia/2024-01/entenda-o-programa-nova-industria-brasil), [G1](https://g1.globo.com/politica/noticia/2024/01/22/nova-politica-industrial-tera-r-300-bilhoes-em-financiamentos-ate-2026-anuncia-governo.ghtml)).

## The expansion of NIB (2024–2026)

Between the launch and the announcement in June 2026, the NIB underwent several expansions:

- **December 2023 (before launch):** MOVER Program — R$ 19.3 billion in tax incentives for automotive decarbonization ([Planalto](https://www.gov.br/planalto/pt-br/acompanhe-o-planalto/noticias/2023/12/mover-novo-programa-amplia-acoes-para-mobilidade-verde-e-descarbonizacao))
- **2024:** Private announcements of R$ 30.8 billion (GM R$ 7 billion, VW R$ 16 billion, BYD R$ 3 billion)
- **September 2024:** Expansion into defense and aerospace
- **December 2024:** Inclusion of strategic mineral chains
- **December 2025:** Embrapii receives R$ 255 million from MCTI ([Embrapii](https://embrapii.org.br/embrapii-e-mcti-anunciam-aporte-de-r-255-milhoes-para-novos-projetos-de-inovacao-e-expansao-da-rede-de-unidades/))

## The announcement of June 22, 2026

### R$ 140 billion additional

The new R$ 140 billion contribution raises the total for NIB to R$ 750 billion (2023–2026). The breakdown is:

| Component | Value |
|------------|-------|
| Initial announcement (Jan/2024) | R$ 300 billion |
| Additional BNDES (Jun/2026) | R$ 106 billion |
| Additional Finep (Jun/2026) | R$ 34 billion |
| Embrapii (Jun/2026) | R$ 1 billion |
| MOVER Program | R$ 19.3 billion |
| **Total** | **~R$ 750 billion** |

### Artificial intelligence as a strategic sector

The inclusion of AI among NIB's priority sectors is a milestone. Until now, AI appeared in industrial policy transversally — within Mission 4 (digital transformation) — but not as a sector with dedicated financing lines. Now, AI companies can access credit from BNDES and Finep with the same favorable conditions as sectors like health and defense ([Finep](https://www.finep.gov.br/w/nova-industria-brasil-tera-r-140-bilhoes-até-dezembro-de-2026)).

According to Aloizio Mercadante, president of BNDES, the new investments "strengthen areas considered strategic for national development, such as artificial intelligence, biopharmaceuticals, and critical minerals" ([BNDES](https://www.bndes.gov.br/wps/portal/site/home/imprensa/noticias/conteudo/bndes-e-finep-anunciam-r-140-bilhoes-adicionais-para-nib)).

### Invest Brazil Industry Portal

The Brazilian Agency for Industrial Development (ABDI) launched, during the event, the Invest Brazil Industry portal — a tool to monitor projects and support companies interested in investing in the segments covered by NIB. The platform will function as a point of articulation between companies and the government, identifying bottlenecks that hinder the implementation of new projects ([Tele.Síntese](https://telesintese.com.br/nova-industria-brasil-recebera-mais-r-140-bilhoes-ate-o-fim-de-2026-ia-esta-entre-os-setores-prioritarios/)).

### Embrapii: record contribution

Another relevant announcement was the R$ 400 million contribution to Embrapii, with a total counterpart of R$ 1 billion (R$ 200 million from BNDES, R$ 200 million from Finep, and R$ 600 million in partnerships). The resources will be allocated to structural research, development, and innovation projects between companies and accredited Embrapii units ([Embrapii](https://embrapii.org.br/aporte-recorde-do-mcti-reforca-orcamento-de-r-1-bilhao-da-embrapii-para-inovacao-em-2026/)).

## What it means to include AI in industrial policy

The decision to include AI as a priority sector has concrete implications:

1.  **Dedicated financing:** Unlike transversal policies (which depend on thematic calls for proposals), AI now has its own credit lines. Brazilian AI companies can access BNDES resources with favorable rates and terms.

2.  **Signal to the market:** The government is saying that AI is not just information technology — it is productive infrastructure comparable to energy, logistics, and health. This attracts private investment.

3.  **Global competition:** The United States (CHIPS Act, US$ 280 billion), the European Union (European Chips Act, € 43 billion), and China (Made in China 2025) already treat digital technology as industrial policy. Brazil enters this game belatedly, but with a volume of resources unprecedented in recent history.

## Challenges

The R$ 750 billion are impressive numbers, but the history of industrial policies in Brazil shows that the bottleneck was never formulation — it was execution.

The Invest Brazil Industry portal, launched by ABDI, is an explicit attempt to solve this problem, functioning as a bottleneck monitoring system. The question remains whether the public sector has the capillarity and agility to transform credit into factories, jobs, and technology by 2026.

BNDES estimates that the NIB financing multiplier is between 2x and 3x — which would mean up to R$ 2.25 trillion in induced investments. If execution matches rhetoric, this could be the largest cycle of industrial investment since PAC.
