The World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) released new data on April 25, 2026 marking World IP Day, and the numbers tell a clear story: **intellectual property in the sports industry is growing at nearly twice the rate of the global IP average.**

Between 2016 and 2025, inventors, brands, and designers filed:

- **65,700+** sports-related patent applications
- **1.25 million+** sports-related trademarks
- **70,000+** sports-related industrial designs

These are not niche numbers — they reflect a structural shift in how the sports industry protects and monetizes innovation.

<!--more-->

## The Growth Rates that Matter

Sports-related IP isn't just large in absolute terms — it's growing faster than virtually every other sector.

| IP Type | Annual Growth (Sports) | Annual Growth (Global Average) | Multiple |
|---------|----------------------|-------------------------------|----------|
| Patents | 7.6% | 4.4% | 1.7x |
| Trademarks | 6.1% | ~3.2% | 1.9x |
| Industrial Designs | 8.3% | ~4.5% | 1.8x |

The pattern is consistent across all three major IP categories. Sports is not a vertical that merely follows the global trend — it is outpacing it decisively.

## Which Sports Lead in Patenting?

Not all sports are equal when it comes to innovation intensity. The WIPO Technology SPARK report identifies **golf, swimming, and racket sports** as the most patent-intensive disciplines.

This makes intuitive sense: these are sports where incremental improvements in equipment — a more aerodynamic golf ball, a faster swimsuit fabric, a lighter tennis racket — translate directly into competitive advantage and commercial value.

**Major patent filers include:**

- **Titleist** and **PING** (golf equipment and ball technology)
- **Nike** and **Adidas** (footwear, apparel, and materials science)
- **Srixon, Decathlon, and Callaway** (top design filers in 2025)

## Trademarks: Where Sports Meets Big Tech

The trademark data reveals something more interesting than patents: the sports IP economy is no longer limited to sports brands.

The top trademark owners in the sports space include **Disney, Apple, and Amazon** — companies whose core business isn't sports, but who have built massive sports-adjacent portfolios through streaming rights, fitness technology, merchandise, and fan engagement platforms.

Alongside them, traditional sports brands like the **NBA, WWE, and Decathlon** remain dominant filers.

This convergence signals a shift: **sports is becoming a content and technology platform**, not just a competition format. The value is shifting from the field to the screen, the wearable, and the data stream.

## Designs: The Consumer-Facing Frontier

In industrial designs, **gym and fitness technologies** lead the filings. This reflects the growing importance of product design and user experience in consumer-facing sports markets.

Top design filers in 2025 were **Srixon** (golf balls), **Decathlon** (multi-sport equipment), and **Callaway** (golf clubs) — all companies where product appearance and user interaction directly influence purchasing decisions.

## Regional Breakdown: Asia Dominates Manufacturing, Europe Leads Branding

| Region | Patents | Designs | Trademarks |
|--------|---------|---------|------------|
| Asia | 63% | 76% | — |
| North America | 22% | — | — |
| Europe | — | — | 43% |

Asia is the top source of sports patents and designs, reflecting its manufacturing and electronics strength. Europe stands out in trademark activity — a testament to the dense landscape of sports brands, clubs, and leagues on the continent.

## The Development Angle: Sports as an IP Gateway

One of the most encouraging findings is the **double-digit growth in sports-related IP in low- and middle-income economies**. WIPO notes that sport can act as an accessible entry point for innovation and IP awareness in developing countries.

For Brazil — a country with a massive sports culture but relatively low IP filing rates per capita — this represents both an opportunity and a diagnostic. The growth of sports-related innovation in developing economies suggests that Brazil's sports industry (football, martial arts, surfing, athletics) could be an underleveraged vector for IP-driven value creation.

## Technologies Reshaping Sport

The WIPO report goes beyond raw numbers and highlights the technologies driving this transformation:

- **Wearable devices** — tracking performance, biometrics, injury prevention
- **Data analytics** — game strategy, player valuation, fan engagement
- **Smart equipment** — connected balls, smart shoes, sensor-enabled gear
- **Officiating technologies** — VAR, automated line calling, AI referees

Many of these innovations are **spilling over from sport into health, entertainment, and consumer products** — a reminder that sports IP has downstream effects beyond the stadium.

## WIPO's Response: A Dedicated Workstream

To step up work in this area, WIPO has formed a **workstream on IP and Sports** and will expand efforts to assist athletes, sports associations, and Member States — including in the area of **e-sports**, which is increasingly recognized as a legitimate IP domain.

## What This Means for Innovators and Practitioners

The data from the WIPO Technology SPARK report tells a clear story: the sports industry is increasingly an IP industry. For patent attorneys, trademark agents, and innovation strategists, this means:

1. **Sports technology is a growing practice area** — the 7.6% annual growth in sports patents is well above the 4.4% global baseline, making it an attractive specialization.
2. **Trademark strategy in sports now requires a multi-industry view** — your client's sports brand competes for attention not just with other sports brands, but with Disney, Apple, and Amazon.
3. **Design protection is as important as patent protection in consumer-facing sports** — the design growth rate (8.3%) is higher than patents (7.6%), reflecting the importance of aesthetics and UX in sports products.
4. **Developing markets are catching up** — the double-digit growth in low- and middle-income economies suggests that the next wave of sports innovation may come from unexpected places.

## Conclusion

The sports IP boom is not a short-term trend — it reflects a structural transformation in how sports are played, consumed, and monetized. As wearables get smarter, data gets richer, and fan engagement gets more immersive, the IP portfolios behind these innovations will only grow.

For Brazil, the window is open. With the world's largest football ecosystem, a growing startup scene, and increasing awareness of IP as a strategic asset, the country has the raw ingredients to capture a share of this booming market.
