The Brazilian Intellectual Property Association (ABPI) presented formal comments to the United States Trade Representative (USTR) in Washington on Monday, July 6, as part of the Section 301 investigation initiated against Brazil. The document responds to preliminary findings released on June 1, 2026, which recommend imposing additional 25% tariffs on select Brazilian products based, among other grounds, on alleged deficiencies in the Brazilian intellectual property (IP) system.

Section 301 is one of the most powerful tools in American trade policy. It allows the USTR to impose unilateral sanctions when it determines that a country's trade practices are unjustifiable or discriminatory and burden U.S. commerce. Brazil has been on the Special 301 Priority Watch List for consecutive years, but this is the first time a formal investigation has advanced to recommending concrete tariff sanctions.

## What ABPI presented to the USTR

Led by Vice President Rodrigo Ouro Preto, ABPI structured its submission around three main pillars.

**Anti-piracy and anti-counterfeiting efforts.** The association highlighted coordinated initiatives by the Federal Revenue Service, the National Council to Combat Piracy (CNCP), the Federal Police, ANATEL, and ANCINE, which have been expanding inspection of counterfeit goods and combating digital piracy. Brazil has implemented new administrative and regulatory measures to strengthen cooperation among IP enforcement authorities.

**INPI's institutional advances.** ABPI presented a comprehensive overview of recent achievements by Brazil's National Institute of Industrial Property (INPI): reduction of the patent application backlog, shorter average examination timelines, recomposition of the examiner workforce, and implementation of technological tools, including artificial intelligence-based solutions. These advances have been consolidated under the National Intellectual Property Strategy (ENPI) and the Interministerial Intellectual Property Group (GIPI), both under the Ministry of Development, Industry, Commerce, and Services (MDIC).

**Priority examination and international cooperation.** The association highlighted expanded priority examination programs and international cooperation with other patent offices to accelerate application review, including in the biopharmaceutical sector — an area of particular interest to U.S. industry.

## The context of INPI's achievements

ABPI's submission to the USTR does not occur in a vacuum. INPI has been delivering concrete results that strengthen Brazil's position in bilateral negotiations.

In June 2026, the Federal Regional Court of the 2nd Region (TRF-2) unanimously upheld a decision requiring the federal government to guarantee structural conditions for INPI's adequate functioning, in a lawsuit filed by ABPI itself — judicial recognition that the institute needs continuous investment to fulfill its mission.

INPI also published, in 2025, its ambitious automation roadmap for 2025-2029, with investments in artificial intelligence, process digitalization, and modernization of patent examination systems. More recently, in July 2026, INPI launched a new version of the Patent Services Module, unifying application filing, annuity payments, and gradually phasing out the old electronic petitioning system.

## What is at stake

The 25% tariffs recommended by the USTR, if implemented, would directly impact Brazilian exports to the United States — one of Brazil's main trading partners. Although the USTR based its recommendation on multiple grounds, intellectual property occupies a central position in the argument.

For the Brazilian IP community, the outcome of this investigation could have long-term consequences:

- **Regulatory pressure.** An unfavorable determination could lead the U.S. to demand legislative or regulatory changes to Brazil's IP system as a condition for removing tariffs, similar to what occurred with Ukraine and Argentina in past Section 301 investigations.

- **Investor signal.** International perception of Brazil's IP environment influences decisions on R&D investment, technology transfer, and innovation center location.

- **Precedent for other sectors.** If IP is used as justification for trade sanctions, other sectors of the Brazilian economy could become targets of similar investigations.

## The ABPI's central argument

ABPI argued that the preliminary report's conclusions "do not adequately reflect the current reality of the country" and that any concerns related to IP protection should be addressed through technical dialogue, bilateral cooperation, and evidence-based assessments — instruments that have historically contributed to strengthening economic and trade relations between Brazil and the United States.

The association reaffirmed that Brazil ensures equal treatment to domestic and foreign IP rights holders, both in administrative and judicial spheres, and that the country has been adopting concrete and continuous measures to improve the system.

## What to watch

- The formal response of the Brazilian government to the Section 301 investigation
- Diplomatic efforts by MDIC and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs with the USTR
- New INPI data on backlog reduction and examination timelines
- Progress of IP discussions under GIPI and ENPI, which may generate new system-strengthening measures
