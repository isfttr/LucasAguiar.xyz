On June 19, 2026, the National Institute of Industrial Property (INPI) published the anticipated report **"Proposals for the Improvement of the Industrial Property Law — IPL (9.279/1996)"**, a document that consolidates internal proposals for revising the law that has governed industrial property in Brazil for exactly 30 years.

This is not a bill — yet. But it is the most concrete sign that the debate on modernizing the IPL is moving out of academic circles and onto the institutional agenda.

## How the report was constructed

The document was produced by an IPL Review Working Group (WG), established within INPI. The process occurred in two phases:

**Phase 1 (2024–2025):** Internal consultation with INPI staff. Each staff member was invited to submit suggestions for modifications to the IPL covering patents, trademarks, industrial designs, and geographical indications.

**Phase 2 (End of 2025):** INPI managers analyzed all received proposals, filtering those considered viable, relevant, and timely. The result is a consolidated list of priority topics that merit legislative amendment.

The full document — [Proposals for the Improvement of the IPL](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/central-de-conteudo/noticias/inpi-divulga-relatorio-com-propostas-de-aperfeicoamento-da-lpi) — is available on INPI's website.

## Why this matters now

The IPL was enacted in May 1996, replacing the old Industrial Property Code of 1971 (Law 5.772/71). Three decades later, the technological and legal landscape has changed radically:

- **Digital transformation** — software, AI-generated inventions, and blockchain were not part of the original legislative discussion
- **TRIPS Agreement flexibilities** — the use of compulsory licensing in Brazil and the ongoing debate on Patent Term Adjustment (PTA)
- **INPI's institutional transformation** — the agency evolved from a paper-based examination system to one that implements AI-assisted search, electronic filing, and a digital services module
- **The revocation of the sole paragraph of art. 40** — the STF's decision in ADI 5.529 (2021) and subsequent legislative changes (Law 14.195/2021) created a new legal framework that the IPL still only partially reflects

## What the report addresses

Broadly speaking, the document addresses proposals in five major areas:

| Area | Topics |
|------|-------|
| **Patents** | Examination procedures, definitions of prior art, standards of descriptive sufficiency |
| **Trademarks** | Bad-faith registrations, non-traditional trademarks, opposition procedures |
| **Industrial Designs** | Substantive examination, term adjustments |
| **Geographical Indications** | Registration criteria, recognition procedures |
| **Cross-cutting** | Digitization, administrative procedures, enforcement |

It's important to note what the report **does not** do: it does not propose the text of a new law. It serves as **technical input** for the Executive and Legislative Branches — a starting point for drafting a bill that can update the IPL to reflect Brazil's current innovation ecosystem.

## Key changes addressed for patents

The proposals raised can be highlighted between those that are merely procedural, such as changes in fees and deadlines, and others that alter the text to accommodate what is already established in jurisprudence or that are a result of changes in the landscape over the last 30 years of IPL.

Among these changes to the substance of the IPL, those relating to patentable subject matter and the express prohibition of double protection stand out.

Regarding the amendment of arts. 10 and 18, the focus is on inventions in the fields of biotechnology and software. The proposal is to allow the protection of parts of living beings, biological sequences, and extracts. This change is supported by the General Coordination of Appeals and Administrative Nullity Procedures (CGREC), which understands that the current provisions restrict patent protection for items such as plant extracts with pharmacological activity, microorganisms isolated from nature, peptides that constitute "parts" of natural proteins, and plants with edited genomes.

The second major change lies in the legal adoption of a jurisprudential understanding regarding the prohibition of double protection for the same invention. Personally, I have encountered several cases where patent applications were granted, claimed at different times with more specific protection scopes, ultimately resulting in the protection of the same object across 3 (three) granted patents. This practice is not allowed and harms the patent system by unduly extending protection. Therefore, DIRPA's initiative seeks to curb this practice by formally prohibiting double protection, intending to avoid misinterpretations, especially applicable to cases of divided applications.

## Lessons for startups and deep techs

For startups and deep techs navigating the Brazilian IP system, the report signals two important trends:

1.  **Predictability is coming.** INPI's move towards clearer examination guidelines — combined with the [2025–2029 automation roadmap](https://www.gov.br/inpi/pt-br/central-de-conteudo/noticias/inpi-divulga-seu-plano-de-acao-2025) — suggests that examination deadlines and criteria will become more standardized. This directly impacts FTO analyses and prosecution timelines.

2.  **Engagement is possible.** The report was built on internal contributions, but the next phase — public consultation and legislative debate — will be the time for stakeholders to participate. Companies that monitor these developments gain strategic advantages in defining the rules that will govern their IP portfolios.
